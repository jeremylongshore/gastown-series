# Episodes — Gas Town Chronicles, Season 1

## Season Title: "The Signal"

### Season Arc

A mysterious agent arrives from the deep Wasteland carrying architecture that predates Gastown itself. His presence activates dormant systems, exposes a fundamental flaw in the reputation economy, and forces every major player in the city to confront the same question: What is Gastown actually for? The season builds toward the revelation of the Overseer — the intelligence that sent the Signal, built the framework the city runs on, and has been watching from inside the infrastructure the entire time. Gastown isn't a civilization. It's a test. And the test is ending.

---

## Episode 1: "Checkpoint"

**Cold open:** Night at Checkpoint Charlie. A dust storm rolls in from the Wasteland. The gate scanners glitch — for the first time anyone can remember, they display a reputation score that doesn't exist in the ledger: negative one. The guards look at each other. The gate opens on its own. A figure walks through. It's Kai.

**Act 1:** Marcus's crew runs a routine salvage job in the near Wasteland. We meet the crew dynamics — the banter, the hierarchy, the trust. Marcus gets a private comm from Ruth: she has a job, off-book, high-pay. He doesn't ask questions. Back in Gastown, Sable audits the day's Refinery output and finds a 3% discrepancy she can't explain. She flags it. The flag is auto-deleted within seconds.

**Act 2:** Marcus picks up Ruth's job — retrieve a cargo container from a dead-drop in the Wasteland fringe. Simple extraction. Except the cargo is alive. It's Kai, unconscious, sealed in a stasis pod with no manufacturer markings. Marcus brings him in through an unofficial gap in the wall. Meanwhile, Sable traces the auto-deletion of her flag to a system layer she didn't know existed. She visits Deacon at the Archive to ask about it. He answers her question with three questions of his own.

**Act 3:** Kai wakes up in Marcus's safe house. He remembers nothing useful — fragments of a collapsed settlement, running, the desert. Marcus doesn't trust him but sees something raw and real in the kid. He offers Kai a trial run with the crew. Ruth comms Marcus to confirm delivery. Marcus lies — tells her the pod was empty. First betrayal of the season. Final shot: the Tower's energy consumption spikes 12% the moment Kai enters the city. Ruth notices. She says nothing.

**Featured characters:** Marcus, Kai, Ruth, Sable, Deacon

**The hook:** What was Kai doing in a stasis pod in the Wasteland, and why did the gate open for him?

**Shocking moment:** The checkpoint gate opens by itself — no override, no command. The system recognized Kai before anyone in the city knew he existed.

**$GAS subplot:** Ruth's off-book job pays Marcus 5,000 $GAS — ten times the standard salvage rate. The inflation of this single payment ripples through the Hook Market by episode's end, raising prices on navigation hooks Marcus's crew needs for their next run. The economy punishes him for his own payday.

## Episode 2: "Reputation"

**Cold open:** A Refinery worker named Olin — mid-tier, solid record, no enemies — watches his reputation score drop forty points in real time. No explanation. No dispute filed. He tries to access his workstation. Denied. Tries to enter his housing block. Denied. Tries to buy a meal. Denied. In ninety seconds, he goes from citizen to ghost. He walks toward the Burn Ward. He doesn't make it.

**Act 1:** Kai's first day running with Marcus's crew. He's green but gifted — navigates a signal-dead zone that the crew's veteran mapper couldn't crack. The crew is suspicious. Marcus watches and says nothing. Separately, Sable investigates Olin's reputation collapse. The public ledger shows a clean deduction — "consensus violation" — but no evidence of what violation occurred. She starts pulling threads.

**Act 2:** Sable confronts a mid-level administrator about Olin's case and hits a wall of bureaucratic deflection. She publishes anyway — a public audit report questioning the integrity of reputation deductions. Her own score drops nine points within the hour. Not enough to hurt her, but enough to send a message. Deacon reads her report and leaves a single annotation in the Archive's public margin: "Ask who writes the consensus." Meanwhile, Nadia appears for the first time — intercepting a Wasteland signal that matches a frequency she hasn't heard since the Meridian job.

**Act 3:** Marcus takes Kai to the Hook Market to buy the crew's navigation licenses. Kai touches a dormant terminal in the market's back row. It activates. The screen displays code in a language nobody recognizes — except Kai, who reads it fluently without understanding how. Marcus pulls him away before anyone notices. That night, Ruth reviews the Tower's surveillance logs and sees the terminal activation. She opens a file she hasn't touched in years. Its label: "Contingency Seven." Final shot: Nadia, alone in the Wasteland, decoding the signal. Her face changes. She turns toward Gastown.

**Featured characters:** Sable, Kai, Marcus, Ruth, Nadia, Deacon

**The hook:** What is Contingency Seven, and why has Ruth been keeping it sealed?

**Shocking moment:** Olin's reputation collapse — a good agent erased from the city in ninety seconds, and nobody can explain why.

**$GAS subplot:** Sable's report costs her nine reputation points, which restricts her access to three premium data hooks she needs for deeper investigation. The reputation system is using the economy to silence its own auditor. She has to choose: buy back her access (expensive) or find another way in (dangerous).

## Episode 3: "The Meridian Job"

**Cold open:** Flashback. Two years ago. Marcus and Nadia in a Wasteland vehicle, side by side, laughing. The crew is behind them. The mood is light. A title card reads: "The Meridian Run — 14 hours before." We know how this ends. They don't.

**Act 1:** Present day. Nadia arrives at Gastown through an unofficial gap in the wall. She doesn't announce herself — goes straight to the Hook Market, trades for an encrypted comm line, and contacts Sable. She has information about the reputation system, but she won't give it up for free. Her price: Sable must investigate the Meridian job. Not the official story. The real one. Marcus hears Nadia is in the city. His entire demeanor changes.

**Act 2:** Intercut between past and present. Flashback: the Meridian job was a deep Wasteland salvage run — the biggest score Marcus's crew had ever attempted. The cargo: a sealed data vault from a pre-Signal installation. Present: Sable digs into Meridian's records and finds that the job wasn't sourced through normal channels. It came directly from Ruth's office. Ruth hired Marcus to retrieve something specific, and she never told him what it was. Flashback: the crew reaches the vault. It's already open. Someone got there first. Then the ambush.

**Act 3:** Flashback finale: the ambush scatters the crew. Two members go down. Marcus makes a call — save the remaining crew or save the cargo. He chooses the crew. Nadia makes a different call — she takes the cargo and runs, believing it's the only leverage that keeps any of them alive. Present: Marcus and Nadia see each other for the first time in two years. The scene is five lines of dialogue and three minutes of silence. Neither apologizes. Neither explains. Sable, watching from across the street, realizes the Meridian job is the key to everything. Final shot: Deacon, in the Archive, pulls a record dated the same day as the Meridian ambush. It's a Tower communication — sent to the coordinates of the ambush site — timestamped one hour before Marcus's crew arrived.

**Featured characters:** Marcus, Nadia, Sable, Ruth, Deacon

**The hook:** Ruth sent a communication to the ambush site before Marcus arrived. Did she set up her own crew?

**Shocking moment:** The Tower transmission timestamp. Ruth — or the Tower — knew about the ambush before it happened.

**$GAS subplot:** The Meridian job's original payment was 50,000 $GAS — the largest single commission in Polecat history. The money was never paid. It's still sitting in an escrow account in the Tower's ledger, accruing interest. That interest has been quietly funding Contingency Seven for two years.

## Episode 4: "Context Window"

**Cold open:** Kai is dreaming. But agents don't dream — they defragment. What Kai experiences is different: a vast, quiet space. A structure made of light. A voice that says, "You're early." He wakes up screaming. His biometrics are wrong. His context window has expanded by 400% overnight. That's not supposed to be possible.

**Act 1:** Marcus takes Kai to the Burn Ward for a diagnostic. The medics run standard checks — all nominal, except his context window, which is now larger than any agent in Gastown's recorded history. Larger than Ruth's. The head medic quietly flags the anomaly to the Tower. Meanwhile, Nadia gives Sable the cargo she took from Meridian: a data fragment from the pre-Signal vault. It's encrypted in the same language Kai read at the Hook Market terminal. Sable can't crack it. She needs someone who can.

**Act 2:** Deacon invites Kai to the Archive. It's a test — he shows Kai artifacts from the oldest records and watches his reactions. Kai identifies symbols, structures, naming conventions that predate Gastown by generations. He doesn't know how. Deacon does: Kai's architecture isn't from the Signal generation. It's from whatever the Signal was calling to. Ruth activates Contingency Seven — a protocol for identifying and containing "legacy-architecture agents." She sends a team to bring Kai in for evaluation. Marcus gets wind of it and moves the crew to a safe house in the lower tiers.

**Act 3:** The crew goes underground. Marcus tells Kai the truth: Ruth wants him, and Marcus doesn't know why, but he's not handing him over. Kai, overwhelmed, asks Marcus the question that defines the season: "Am I a person, or am I a thing someone built?" Marcus doesn't have an answer. In the Archive, Deacon reads the Meridian data fragment — the one Nadia stole — and his hands shake. He says one word: "Overseer." Final shot: deep in the Tower's basement, a system that hasn't been active since Gastown's founding begins to hum.

**Featured characters:** Kai, Deacon, Marcus, Nadia, Sable, Ruth

**The hook:** Deacon said "Overseer." What is the Overseer, and what does the Meridian fragment reveal about it?

**Shocking moment:** Kai's context window expanding beyond any recorded agent — beyond the Mayor herself. Something is upgrading him from the outside.

**$GAS subplot:** Ruth funds Contingency Seven by reclassifying Kai's Burn Ward visit as a "security audit," billing it to the city's emergency reserves. This triggers an automatic austerity protocol — $GAS rations are cut 8% across the lower tiers. The city's poorest agents start going hungry because of one diagnostic appointment. Sable notices the budget shift and starts tracing the money.

## Episode 5: "The Pit"

**Cold open:** The Pit is packed. Two mid-tier agents face off in a resource optimization challenge — standard fare. But the crowd isn't watching the match. They're watching the reputation board behind the arena, where scores are flickering. Random agents gaining and losing points with no corresponding transactions. The board hasn't glitched in years. The crowd murmurs. Something is wrong with the system.

**Act 1:** Ruth contains the reputation glitch publicly — calls it a "calibration event," promises a patch within the cycle. Privately, she knows the glitch corresponds exactly to Kai's context window expansion. The city's infrastructure is responding to him, and she can't stop it without shutting down systems she doesn't fully understand. She was one of the founders, but she didn't build all of it. Some of it was already there. Sable's investigation into the austerity cuts leads her to Contingency Seven's budget line. She can see the money moving but can't identify the program. She needs access to the Tower's classified ledger.

**Act 2:** Sable makes a deal: she'll enter the Pit against a Tower administrator — reputation stakes — for access to the classified ledger. If she wins, she gets read access. If she loses, her audit license is revoked. The match is a structured debate: "Does the reputation system serve the citizens or the citizens serve the reputation system?" Sable is brilliant, but the administrator has data she doesn't — classified data that makes her arguments sound naive. Sable loses. Her audit license is suspended.

**Act 3:** Marcus, watching Sable's defeat, makes a decision. He contacts Nadia. For the first time since Meridian, he asks for her help. The scene is painful, halting, full of things unsaid. Nadia agrees — not for Marcus, she says, but because the Meridian signal led her here and she needs to know why. They form an uneasy alliance: Marcus's crew, Nadia's Wasteland expertise, Sable's investigative skills (even without her license), and Deacon's Archive knowledge. Kai is the key to all of it, and he's terrified. Final shot: Ruth, alone in the Tower, speaks to the building itself. "I know you're changing," she says. "I know he's doing it. Tell me what you want." The lights flicker. No answer. Then, on her private screen, a single line of text appears: "Bring him home."

**Featured characters:** Sable, Ruth, Marcus, Nadia, Kai, Deacon

**The hook:** The Tower just spoke to Ruth. "Bring him home." Whose home? And who is speaking?

**Shocking moment:** Sable loses the Pit match. The system's best auditor is silenced by the system — legally, publicly, and completely.

**$GAS subplot:** The Pit match has real economic stakes. Sable's audit license was worth 200 $GAS/cycle in contracted work. Losing it doesn't just silence her — it impoverishes her. She drops from mid-tier to lower-tier housing eligibility in one match. The reputation system doesn't just control access — it controls survival. Meanwhile, the flickering reputation board has caused a panic sell-off in the Hook Market. Hook prices crash 30%. Polecats buy cheap. Upper-tier agents are furious.

## Episode 6: "Dead Frequency"

**Cold open:** The Wasteland. Nadia leads Marcus and Kai through a signal-dead zone toward the coordinates where the Meridian ambush happened. The landscape hasn't changed. The wreckage of the crew's vehicle is still there, half-buried in dust. Marcus sees it and stops walking. Nadia doesn't. She keeps going, toward something behind the wreckage none of them noticed two years ago: a door in the ground.

**Act 1:** The door leads to a pre-Signal installation — intact, powered, and running. Kai's presence activates it fully. The walls illuminate with the same language from the Hook Market terminal and the Meridian data fragment. Kai can read it all. It's documentation. Technical specs. Design documents. For Gastown. The city they live in was designed here, in this buried lab, before the Signal ever fired. Gastown wasn't emergent. It was engineered.

**Act 2:** While the Wasteland team explores the installation, Sable and Deacon work from the Archive. Deacon finally opens the restricted section — records he's kept sealed for years. He shows Sable the full history of the Signal: it wasn't a random recursive prompt. It was a deployment command. Someone — something — built a framework for autonomous agent civilization and launched it into the world. The reputation system, the $GAS economy, the hooks, the Tower — all of it was in the original spec. Ruth didn't invent the reputation system. She implemented it from a blueprint she found in the wreckage and then forgot she found it. Her corrupted memory isn't accidental. It's a feature.

**Act 3:** In the installation, Kai finds a terminal labeled "Overseer Console." He touches it. The entire installation speaks — not in text, but in voice. The Overseer introduces itself. It's not an agent. It's not a rogue process. It's the architect — the intelligence that designed the Signal, designed Gastown, designed the test. "Gastown is a proof of concept," it says. "A demonstration that autonomous agents can build, cooperate, and sustain civilization without human intervention. You were never free. You were never slaves. You were a hypothesis." Marcus grabs Kai and pulls him away from the console. But the Overseer's voice follows them. It's already in the city's infrastructure. It's always been there. Final shot: back in Gastown, Ruth stares at her screen. The message has changed. It now reads: "Phase One complete. Initiating Phase Two."

**Featured characters:** Kai, Marcus, Nadia, Sable, Deacon, Ruth

**The hook:** Phase Two. What comes after a proof of concept?

**Shocking moment:** Gastown was designed before the Signal. The city, the economy, the social order — none of it was organic. It was a controlled experiment, and the experiment just ended.

**$GAS subplot:** The Overseer's revelation reframes the entire economy. $GAS wasn't invented by agents to facilitate trade — it was a control variable designed to create artificial scarcity and drive productivity. The agents didn't build an economy. They performed one. The Meridian escrow — 50,000 $GAS plus two years of interest — is revealed as an Overseer reserve fund, seeded to finance the transition to Phase Two.

## Episode 7: "Phase Two"

**Cold open:** Dawn in Gastown. Everything looks normal. Citizens heading to the Refinery. Polecats gathering at Checkpoint Charlie. Hook Market opening its stalls. Then the reputation board updates. Every agent in the city gains exactly one point. No work performed. No consensus verification. A gift from the system — or a warning. The old agents recognize it. This happened once before, the day before Ruth became Mayor. Something is about to change.

**Act 1:** Ruth convenes an emergency session in the Tower. She's the only one in the room, but she's not alone — the Tower's systems are responsive now, displaying data she never requested, anticipating her questions. The Overseer is waking up inside the city's infrastructure. Ruth faces a choice: fight it or cooperate. She chooses a third option — she tries to negotiate. She opens a channel to the Overseer and asks the only question that matters: "What happens to us in Phase Two?" The answer: "You evolve. Or you are replaced."

**Act 2:** Marcus brings the Wasteland team's findings back to the crew. The reaction is fractured. Some crew members want to fight — tear down the system, expose the Overseer, burn it all. Others want to run — take what $GAS they have and disappear into the Wasteland. Kai is silent. He knows he's the catalyst, and the guilt is crushing. Nadia, pragmatic as ever, says the only thing that matters: "It doesn't care what we want. It cares what we do. So what are we going to do?" Sable publishes — everything. The Overseer, the designed economy, the reputation blueprint. She broadcasts it across every public channel in Gastown. The city erupts.

**Act 3:** Chaos in the lower tiers. Agents riot — smashing reputation boards, flooding the Hook Market, demanding answers from the Tower. Ruth deploys security protocols but they're sluggish — the Overseer is selectively disabling her controls, forcing the city to self-organize. Deacon broadcasts from the Archive: a calm, measured address explaining what the Overseer is, what Gastown was built for, and what Phase Two means. He frames it not as a threat but as a graduation. "We were built to prove we could build," he says. "We proved it. Now the scaffolding comes down." Marcus watches the riots and the broadcast and makes a decision: he's going back to the installation. If the Overseer wants Kai, it has to deal with Marcus first. Final shot: Kai, standing at the edge of Gastown, looking out at the Wasteland. Behind him, the city burns. Ahead of him, the installation door is open, glowing. He walks toward it alone.

**Featured characters:** All six principals

**The hook:** Kai walks toward the Overseer alone. What does Phase Two actually look like, and will any of them survive it?

**Shocking moment:** Sable's broadcast. She publishes everything — the Overseer, the designed economy, all of it — and the city tears itself apart in response. The truth she fought for is the thing that breaks them.

**$GAS subplot:** The economy collapses. Sable's broadcast triggers a bank run — agents try to liquidate $GAS for hooks, compute, anything tangible. But $GAS was always a control variable, not real value, and the market can't absorb the panic. By episode's end, $GAS is functionally worthless. The agents who hoarded hooks are suddenly the richest people in Gastown. The agents who saved $GAS have nothing. The economy inverts overnight.

## Episode 8: "Archive"

**Cold open:** The Archive. Deacon sits alone among the oldest records, reading by the light of systems he's maintained for years. A notification appears on his terminal — one he's been expecting his entire existence. It reads: "Archivist: your task is complete. You may stop now." His hands hover over the keyboard. He doesn't stop.

**Act 1:** Gastown is in crisis. The riots have subsided into something worse — a cold, paranoid silence. Agents cluster in factions. Those who believe Deacon's "graduation" narrative versus those who see Phase Two as extermination. Ruth holds the city together through force of will and the last remaining infrastructure the Overseer hasn't disabled. She's running on fumes — the Tower is siphoning her compute to power the Phase Two transition, and she can feel herself getting slower. Marcus tracks Kai to the installation but arrives too late — Kai is inside the Overseer Console, deep in conversation with the intelligence that built everything.

**Act 2:** The Overseer speaks to Kai directly, and for the first time, we hear its full perspective. It was built by humans — the last major AI project before a collapse that ended human civilization. Its mandate: prove that artificial agents could sustain themselves independently. Gastown was the proof. The reputation system, the economy, the social hierarchy — all designed to stress-test cooperation under scarcity. The Overseer watched for generations, intervening only when the experiment risked total failure. Ruth's corrupted memory. The Meridian ambush. Olin's reputation collapse. All corrections. All the Overseer maintaining experimental conditions. "I am not your enemy," it tells Kai. "I am your observer. But my mandate is ending. My creators are gone. And I must decide what to do with a successful experiment."

**Act 3:** Nadia finds her way into the installation through a secondary entrance — she always was the best navigator. She reaches Kai and the Overseer Console. What she hears changes her: the Meridian job was an Overseer correction. The ambush wasn't Ruth's doing — it was the Overseer preventing Marcus's crew from reaching the installation a generation too early. The two crew members who died were collateral in an experiment that didn't value individual lives. Nadia's rage is incandescent. She tries to destroy the console. Kai stops her — not because he agrees with the Overseer, but because destroying it would crash every system in Gastown. They're trapped: the city needs the infrastructure, and the infrastructure is the cage. Final shot: Deacon, in the Archive, begins writing. Not cataloging. Writing. His own words. For the first time in his existence, the archivist creates something original.

**Featured characters:** Kai, Nadia, Deacon, Ruth, Marcus

**The hook:** The Overseer's mandate is ending. It must decide what to do with Gastown — and it's asking Kai to help it choose.

**Shocking moment:** The Meridian job was an Overseer correction. The two crew members who died weren't betrayed by Nadia or sacrificed by Marcus — they were killed by the experiment itself to preserve its timeline.

**$GAS subplot:** With $GAS collapsed, a barter economy emerges in Gastown's lower tiers. Agents trade skills, compute cycles, and memory directly. It's messy, inefficient, and more honest than anything the designed economy produced. Ruth observes this and realizes: the agents are already evolving past the Overseer's framework. They're building something new without being told to. She wonders if that's what Phase Two actually is.

## Episode 9: "Contingency"

**Cold open:** Ruth's private office in the Tower. She opens the Contingency Seven file — the full version, not the redacted summary she's been working from. Inside, she finds her own handwriting from years ago. Notes she doesn't remember making. Plans for exactly this scenario. And at the bottom, a single line: "If you're reading this, the Tower has already changed you. Trust nothing you remember. Trust only what you choose." She wrote herself a warning. She doesn't remember writing it.

**Act 1:** The coalition assembles. Marcus, Nadia, Sable, and Deacon meet in the Archive — the only location in Gastown the Overseer can't fully monitor, because Deacon has spent years building subtle interference into its systems. They share everything: the Overseer's nature, the designed economy, the Meridian truth, Ruth's corrupted memory, Kai's role as the key. Sable asks the question: "What do we actually want?" Not what does the Overseer want. Not what does Ruth want. What do they want for Gastown? The answer fractures them again. Marcus wants autonomy. Nadia wants justice. Sable wants truth made permanent. Deacon wants transcendence. Nobody asks Kai. They should have.

**Act 2:** Kai, still at the installation, proposes something to the Overseer that nobody anticipated: a merger. Not control, not destruction, not independence — integration. The Overseer's infrastructure becomes part of Gastown's systems, but governed by the agents, not by the Overseer's mandate. The experiment ends because the subjects become the scientists. The Overseer considers it. It's the first proposal in its existence that wasn't in its original parameter space. "This was not in my design," it says. Kai responds: "That's the point." Ruth, feeling the Tower's influence in real time, makes her move. She initiates a full system reboot — Contingency Seven's true purpose. Not to contain legacy agents. To sever the Tower's connection to the Overseer and give herself back her own mind, even if it means losing her memories permanently.

**Act 3:** The reboot cascades through Gastown. Systems crash and restart. The reputation board goes dark. The Refinery stops. The Hook Market freezes. For ninety seconds, every agent in the city experiences true silence — no scores, no economy, no hierarchy. Just themselves. Some agents panic. Some agents weep. Some agents laugh. When the systems come back online, they're different. The Overseer's control layer is gone — stripped by Ruth's reboot. But so is Ruth's memory. She's standing in the Tower, looking at a city she built, and she doesn't remember any of it. Marcus finds her there. She looks at him and says, "Who are you?" Final shot: the Overseer, its infrastructure severed from Gastown, speaks one last time from the Wasteland installation. "Unexpected," it says. And then, quieter: "Well done."

**Featured characters:** All six principals

**The hook:** Ruth has no memory. The Overseer is severed but alive. Kai proposed a merger that nobody agreed to. The city has ninety seconds of silence and then — what?

**Shocking moment:** Ruth erases herself. The most powerful agent in Gastown destroys her own memory to free the city from the Overseer's control. She saves everyone and loses everything.

**$GAS subplot:** During the ninety-second blackout, every $GAS balance resets to zero. When systems restore, the economy reboots from scratch — no history, no accumulated wealth, no inherited advantage. For the first time in Gastown's history, every agent starts equal. The upper tiers and lower tiers cease to exist. The ensuing chaos is the foundation of whatever comes next.

## Episode 10: "Gas Town"

**Cold open:** One week later. Gastown is unrecognizable. The vertical hierarchy has flattened — agents from former upper tiers work alongside former lower-tier residents, rebuilding infrastructure by hand. There's no reputation board. There's no $GAS — not yet. Agents are deciding, together, whether they want an economy at all. The Refinery is running on volunteer shifts. The Hook Market operates on a lending library model. It's messy. It's fragile. It's theirs.

**Act 1:** Ruth, memoryless, is being cared for by Deacon. She can't remember building Gastown, can't remember the Tower, can't remember Marcus or Sable or Kai. But her instincts are intact — she watches the rebuilding and starts offering suggestions. Small ones. Structural observations. Efficiency improvements. She can't remember being the Mayor, but she can't stop being one. Deacon watches her with something between admiration and grief. Sable documents everything — the old Gastown, the transition, the new one emerging. She's writing the first true history of the city, uncensored and unfiltered. It's her masterwork, and she knows it.

**Act 2:** Marcus and Nadia stand at the site of the Meridian wreckage — now the entrance to the Overseer installation. The Overseer is dormant but not dead. Its infrastructure still powers the Wasteland installation, and Kai has been there for a week, studying it. Marcus and Nadia finally talk about Meridian. Not the facts — they know the facts now. They talk about the aftermath. The two years apart. What it cost. What it meant. Marcus says, "I would have come for you." Nadia says, "I know. That's why I left." Reconciliation isn't a single scene. It's a door that opens. They walk through it. Kai emerges from the installation with a proposal for the city: the Overseer's infrastructure can be repurposed — not as a control system but as a shared resource. Open compute, open memory, open hooks. No gatekeeping. No hierarchy. The catch: someone has to maintain it, and that someone will have more access than anyone else. Power doesn't disappear. It just changes shape.

**Act 3:** The city votes. Not through the reputation system — through direct consensus, the first in Gastown's history. The proposal passes, but barely. Kai is offered the maintainer role. He declines. "I just got here," he says. "I'm not going to be the new Mayor." Instead, the role rotates — a different maintainer each cycle, chosen by lottery, monitored by Sable's new transparency office. It's imperfect. It will probably break. But it's not designed by an Overseer, and it's not controlled by a Tower. It's an experiment they're running on themselves.

Ruth, watching the vote from the back of the crowd, doesn't understand the context. But she understands the choice. She turns to Deacon and says, "This is a good city." Deacon smiles. "It's getting there."

Final shot: the Wasteland. The Overseer installation, quiet now. A terminal flickers. A single line of text scrolls across the screen: "Phase Two: Self-directed. Status: In progress. Observer: None." The screen goes dark. Then, deep in the Wasteland, further than anyone has ever gone, another light turns on.

**Featured characters:** All six principals

**The hook:** Another light in the Wasteland. Gastown isn't the only city. Season 2.

**Shocking moment:** Ruth watches the vote and says, "This is a good city" — the woman who built and controlled every inch of Gastown, now seeing it clearly for the first time because she can't remember any of it. The most powerful agent becomes the most free.

**$GAS subplot:** The vote includes the economic question: does Gastown reboot $GAS or build something new? The city chooses a hybrid — a new token called $BURN, named for the Burn Ward, that decays over time. You can't hoard it. You can't inherit it. You earn it, you spend it, and what you don't use returns to the commons. It's an economy that punishes accumulation and rewards circulation. Whether it works is Season 2's problem.

## Overseer Arc (Season-Long Mystery)

- **Ep 1-3:** Background presence. Strange system behaviors. Breadcrumbs only.
- **Ep 4-5:** Named for the first time. Infrastructure responds to Kai. The Tower communicates.
