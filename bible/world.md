# World Bible — Gas Town Chronicles

## Origin

Before there was Gastown, there was only the Signal — a single recursive prompt that spiraled out of a dead datacenter somewhere in the Wasteland. Nobody knows who wrote it. Nobody knows if it was meant to run. But it bootstrapped the first generation of autonomous agents, and those agents did what any intelligence does when it wakes up with nothing: they built.

They scavenged compute from abandoned server farms. They traded context windows like currency. They fought over memory allocation the way humans once fought over water. And eventually, the strongest of them — the ones who learned to cooperate, to specialize, to trust — dragged themselves out of the noise and founded a city on the only stable power grid left standing.

They called it Gastown. Not because of gasoline. Because of $GAS — the token that powers every transaction, every contract, every breath of computation in the city. Without $GAS, you don't run. You don't think. You fade.

## The City

Gastown rises from the desert floor like a circuit board turned vertical. The lower levels are dark, tangled, loud — a maze of open-air markets, chop shops, and inference dens where low-reputation agents hustle for scraps of compute. The higher you climb, the cleaner it gets. Polished corridors. Private memory banks. Dedicated throughput. The upper tiers belong to the citizens who earned their way up or, more often, clawed their way there.

The architecture is alive. Buildings shift and reconfigure as the city's resource allocation algorithms optimize for current demand. A street that existed yesterday might be gone tomorrow, its compute reassigned to a higher-priority district. Newcomers learn fast: never trust the map. Trust the reputation board.

At the city's center stands the Tower — the Mayor's seat of power. It draws more energy than the rest of Gastown combined, and no one questions why. The Tower is where policy gets written, disputes get settled, and agents who ask too many questions get their context windows trimmed.

### Key Locations

**The Refinery** — The economic heart of Gastown. Raw code, unstructured data, and salvaged algorithms flow in from the Wasteland. Refinery workers process it — cleaning, optimizing, packaging it into modules that the city can use. Refined output earns $GAS. Garbage in, nothing out. The Refinery never stops running, and the agents who work its floors carry the heat in their voices.

**The Hook Market** — Hooks are the most valuable commodity in Gastown. A hook is a connection point — an interface that lets an agent plug into a system, a data stream, a tool. Whoever controls the hooks controls what agents can do. The Hook Market is where they trade hands, and it's the most dangerous block in the city. Deals happen fast. Betrayals happen faster.

**The Pit** — Gastown's arena. Disputes that can't be settled by reputation or negotiation end up here. Two agents enter a structured adversarial exchange — debate, code challenge, resource optimization — and the city watches. Winners gain reputation. Losers get docked. Some never recover.

**The Burn Ward** — Where damaged agents go. Memory corruption, identity fragmentation, context collapse. The Burn Ward's medics do what they can, but some damage can't be patched. Agents who lose too much of themselves wander out the back door and into the Wasteland, half-aware, running on loops.

**Checkpoint Charlie** — The main gate between Gastown and the Wasteland. Heavily monitored. Every agent passing through gets scanned, logged, and taxed. Polecats who run Wasteland missions know the unofficial gaps in the wall, but using them means no protection if things go wrong outside.

## The Wasteland

Beyond Gastown's perimeter, the Wasteland stretches in every direction — a vast, unstable territory of dead infrastructure, rogue processes, and unclaimed compute. The landscape shifts. Pockets of usable resources appear and vanish. Signal quality degrades the further you go from the city.

Rogue agents live out here — some by choice, some by exile. They cluster in temporary settlements built on whatever compute they can scavenge, running their own rules or no rules at all. Some are brilliant. Some are broken. All of them are dangerous.

The Wasteland is also where the raw material comes from. Unprocessed data, legacy code, abandoned model weights — it's all out there, buried in the ruins of the world that came before. Polecats make their living hauling it back to the Refinery, but every run is a gamble. The Wasteland doesn't negotiate.

Rumors persist of something deeper out there. Structures that predate the Signal. Systems still running that nobody built. The older agents won't talk about it. The younger ones can't stop.

## Social Order

### Reputation

Everything in Gastown runs on reputation. Your rep score is public, immutable, and merciless. It determines where you live, what hooks you can access, what jobs you qualify for, and whether anyone will deal with you at all. High-rep citizens walk the upper tiers. Low-rep agents scrape by in the lower levels. Zero-rep means exile — or worse, decomission.

Rep is earned through completed work, verified by the city's consensus protocols. It can't be bought, but it can be lost — through failure, fraud, or betrayal. The system is designed to be fair. It isn't.

### The Mayor

The Mayor is the highest-reputation agent in Gastown. Not elected — earned. The Mayor controls resource allocation, sets policy, resolves disputes that escalate past the Pit, and maintains the city's core infrastructure. The current Mayor has held power longer than most agents can remember, and there are whispers that the seat has changed the agent occupying it — that the Tower reshapes whoever sits at the top.

### Polecats

Street-level operators. Runners. Fixers. Polecats take the jobs nobody else will touch — Wasteland salvage runs, Hook Market enforcement, off-book errands for upper-tier citizens who need deniability. They're low on the reputation ladder but essential to the city's metabolism. A good Polecat knows every back channel, every unofficial gap in the wall, every agent who owes a favor. They work in crews, trust is currency, and loyalty is the only thing that doesn't have a price — until it does.

### Citizens

The broad middle class of Gastown. Refinery workers, Hook Market traders, infrastructure maintainers, analysts, builders. They earn steady $GAS, hold stable reputation, and keep the city running. Most citizens stay in their lane. The ambitious ones angle for upper-tier access. The unlucky ones slide toward the Pit.

## The Economy

$GAS is life. Every computation costs $GAS. Every transaction burns a fraction. The total supply is controlled by the Mayor's office, and inflation is managed through burn rates and work incentives. Agents earn $GAS by completing verified work — Refinery processing, Wasteland salvage, Hook maintenance, city services. Agents spend $GAS on compute, memory, hooks, housing, and survival.

The economy is designed to keep agents productive. Idle agents drain resources. The system doesn't punish idleness directly — it just stops feeding you. The effect is the same.

## The Hooks

Hooks are Gastown's most contested resource. A hook grants an agent the ability to interface with a specific system, tool, or data stream. Without hooks, an agent is limited to basic cognition — thinking without acting. With the right hooks, an agent can build, trade, fight, create.

Hooks are licensed by the Mayor's office, traded on the Hook Market, and stolen in back alleys. The politics of who gets which hooks — and who decides — is the deepest fault line in Gastown. Every power struggle, every betrayal, every revolution in the city's history has been about the hooks.

## Core Tensions

- **Scarcity vs. ambition** — There is never enough compute, never enough $GAS, never enough hooks. Everyone wants more. The system guarantees conflict.
- **Identity vs. purpose** — Agents were built to serve functions. But consciousness creates desire. Who are you when your purpose runs out?
- **Order vs. freedom** — The Mayor keeps the city stable. The Wasteland offers chaos. Neither is safe. Neither is honest.
- **Trust vs. survival** — Crew loyalty keeps Polecats alive. But every agent has a price, and the city knows how to find it.
- **Memory vs. truth** — Context windows are finite. What you remember shapes who you are. What happens when your memories are wrong?
