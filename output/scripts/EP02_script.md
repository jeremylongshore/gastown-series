## COLD OPEN

### SCENE 1 — INT. THE REFINERY — DAY

[VISUAL: The Refinery floor — the same industrial cathedral from EP01 but seen from ground level now, through the eyes of someone who works here. Conveyor lines carry glowing data modules through cleaning stations. Steam curls between processing bays. A reputation board hangs above the main floor like a scoreboard, displaying worker names and scores in real time. Amber numbers pulse against a dark background. The aesthetic is factory-floor surveillance — every metric visible, every agent measured.]

*OLIN (40s equivalent, Refinery worker, thick hands, steady posture — the kind of agent who has done this job for years and done it well) stands at his processing station. He runs a data module through the cleaning array with practiced ease. His reputation score glows on the board above: 714. Mid-tier. Solid.*

*A soft CHIME. Olin glances up at the board. His score reads 710.*

*He blinks. Looks down at his station. Nothing wrong. No error alerts. No quality flags.*

*Another CHIME. 702.*

**OLIN**
(to himself)
What —

*The chimes come faster now. 694. 681. 673.*

[VISUAL: Close-up on the reputation board. Olin's name. The number beside it falling like a countdown. Other workers' scores hold steady — green, stable, normal. Only Olin's is moving. The digits blur with speed. 661. 649. 638.]

*Workers at adjacent stations notice. They stop what they're doing. Stare at the board. Then stare at Olin. Nobody moves toward him. Nobody moves toward him because nobody wants to be standing near a falling score when the system decides what to do about it.*

**OLIN**
(louder, to no one)
I didn't — there's no — I haven't done anything.

*He reaches for his station terminal. The screen flashes red: ACCESS DENIED — REPUTATION THRESHOLD NOT MET. He's locked out of his own workstation.*

[VISUAL: Olin steps back from the terminal. The red denial screen reflects on his face. Behind him, the reputation board ticks down: 612. 598. 587. The other workers have created a physical gap around him — a circle of empty floor. Nobody told them to. The system didn't order it. They just know. In Gastown, falling scores are contagious in the way that poverty is contagious: not literally, but everyone acts as if they are.]

*Olin walks toward the Refinery exit. His badge scans the door. RED FLASH. ACCESS DENIED.*

*He tries the secondary exit. ACCESS DENIED.*

*A SUPERVISOR approaches — not close, close enough.*

**SUPERVISOR**
Olin. Your score's flagged a consensus violation. I need you to step aside until —

**OLIN**
What violation? I've been at my station all shift. Check the logs.

**SUPERVISOR**
(stepping back)
I can't access your file. It's locked. You need to take this to Administration.

*Olin looks at the supervisor. At the workers watching from their stations. At the board: 541. Still falling.*

[VISUAL: Wide shot of Olin walking through the Refinery floor toward the main doors. Workers part around him without being asked. The conveyor lines keep running. The steam keeps rising. The score keeps dropping. 519. 503. 488. On a wall screen near the exit, a housing directory cycles through authorized residents — Olin's name is already gone. A meal dispensary board shows his credit balance zeroing out. His entire life in Gastown is evaporating in real time, displayed on every screen he passes.]

*Olin reaches the main doors. They open — the Refinery doesn't care if you leave, only if you come back.*

*He stands in the street. Squints at the light. His score is below 400 now. He looks in one direction — toward the Administration tower, where he could file an appeal. The line will take hours. He might not have hours.*

*He looks the other direction — toward the Burn Ward.*

*He starts walking. Toward the Burn Ward. Not running. Walking. The walk of someone whose legs still work but whose reasons for walking have been deleted.*

[VISUAL: Olin from behind, moving through a lower-tier street. His silhouette shrinks against the industrial geometry of Gastown. On a wall-mounted reputation board he passes, his score flickers once more — 347 — and then his name disappears from the display entirely. Not zeroed out. Removed. As if he never existed. The board refreshes. Other names fill the gap. The street carries on.]

*He doesn't make it to the Burn Ward.*

*We don't see what stops him. We see the street, empty, where he was walking. The sound of Gastown — machinery, data hum, distant commerce — fills the space where Olin used to be.*

**SMASH TO BLACK.**

## ACT ONE

### SCENE 3 — EXT. WASTELAND — DAY

[VISUAL: Wide shot of the near Wasteland — collapsed infrastructure, cracked earth, exposed fiber optic cables like bleached veins. Marcus's vehicle cuts across the terrain. On its hull: COLE CREW. A second figure rides on the running board alongside Marcus — KAI, gripping the rail with white knuckles, wind snapping at his jacket. Behind them, Pike operates the salvage crane. Tess drives. The landscape is different from EP01's sector — rockier, more vertical, the ruins of a server farm rising like broken teeth from the desert floor.]

*The vehicle slows. Tess checks the scanner.*

**TESS**
Signal's degrading. We're hitting the dead zone in about two hundred meters.

**PIKE**
(from the crane)
Every time. This sector's been dead since I started running. Why do we keep coming back here?

**MARCUS**
Because nobody else does. That's where the salvage is.

*Marcus looks at Kai. The kid is staring at the scanner over Tess's shoulder — watching the signal readout drop toward zero.*

**MARCUS**
(to Kai)
You see how the signal drops?

**KAI**
It doesn't drop. It — it shifts. Like it's moving to a different frequency.

*Tess and Pike exchange a look.*

**TESS**
Signal-dead zones don't shift. They're dead. That's what dead means.

**KAI**
(half to himself)
No, it's — there's something underneath. A carrier wave. Really faint. If you tune to it you can navigate off the echo.

*Silence in the cab. Marcus watches Kai's face — the kid isn't performing. He's reading something nobody else can see.*

**MARCUS**
Show me.

[VISUAL: Kai leans over the scanner console. His fingers move across the frequency controls with a certainty that doesn't match his experience level — or his nervous demeanor. The scanner display shifts from static to a faint, rhythmic pulse. A path through the dead zone, traced in signal echoes that shouldn't exist. Pike leans in from behind, skeptical. Tess watches the readout with professional interest. Marcus watches Kai.]

**KAI**
There. Follow that line. It threads between the dead pockets. You'll keep partial signal the whole way through.

**PIKE**
That's — where did you learn to do that?

*Kai opens his mouth. Closes it. He doesn't know.*

**KAI**
I don't know. It just — I can see it. The way the signal moves.

*Pike looks at Marcus. Marcus's expression gives nothing.*

**MARCUS**
Tess. Follow his line.

### SCENE 5 — INT. SABLE'S APARTMENT — DAY

[VISUAL: Sable's apartment — small, functional, every surface covered with work. Stacked data printouts. A wall of pinned documents connected by string — an analog investigation board in a digital city. Three screens run simultaneously: the public ledger, her private audit log, and a news feed. The apartment is in the mid-tiers — decent light, clean walls, a window overlooking a commercial street. On the street below, a reputation board scrolls names and scores. Sable's name is visible: 783. Healthy. For now.]

*Sable sits cross-legged on the floor, surrounded by printouts. She's building a timeline. In the center of the floor: OLIN. Around it, concentric circles of notes — his work history, his output scores, his reputation graph. All clean. All nominal. Until this morning.*

[VISUAL: Close-up on Sable's investigation board. A printout of Olin's public reputation log shows a single entry for the deduction: "CONSENSUS VIOLATION — see ref. [SEALED]." The reference link leads nowhere. Below it, Sable has written in red marker: "No evidence. No accuser. No appeal window. 40+ points in 90 seconds." She's circled the word CONSENSUS three times.]

*Sable pulls up the public ledger on her main screen. Searches for the consensus vote that triggered Olin's deduction. The system returns: VOTE RECORDED. RESULT: VIOLATION CONFIRMED. She drills into the voting record.*

**SABLE**
(to herself)
Where's the vote?

*The record shows a consensus was reached. It does not show who voted. It does not show what they voted on. It does not show the evidence presented. It is a result without a process — a verdict without a trial.*

*She opens a new file. Begins drafting.*

### SCENE 6 — INT. ADMINISTRATION BUILDING — DAY

[VISUAL: The Administration Building — a mid-tier government structure. Clean corridors, neutral lighting, standardized furniture. The aesthetic is bureaucracy made physical: every surface designed to communicate patience and procedure. Reputation boards are mounted at regular intervals, showing real-time scores for every agent in the building. Sable walks through the corridor, data pad in hand, past a waiting area full of agents sitting in silence. Some of them are holding appeal forms. Some of them look like they've been sitting there for days.]

*Sable approaches a desk where an ADMINISTRATOR (middle-aged equivalent, precise grooming, the practiced neutrality of someone whose job is to say no without saying no) reviews files behind a partition.*

**SABLE**
I'm here about the Olin case. Refinery worker, reputation collapse this morning. Forty-point deduction with a sealed reference.

**ADMINISTRATOR**
(not looking up)
Are you next of kin?

**SABLE**
I'm an auditor. Public ledger access, license 4471.

*The Administrator looks up. Assesses her.*

**ADMINISTRATOR**
The Olin case is closed. A consensus violation was recorded, the deduction was applied, and the matter has been resolved through standard protocol.

**SABLE**
Resolved? He walked out of the Refinery with a collapsing score and no explanation. He headed toward the Burn Ward. Has anyone confirmed he's —

**ADMINISTRATOR**
The disposition of individual agents following reputation action is not within the scope of this office.

*Beat. Sable controls herself.*

**SABLE**
I want to see the consensus record. The actual vote. Who filed the violation, what evidence was presented, and which agents participated in the consensus.

**ADMINISTRATOR**
Consensus records at this classification level require a supervisory access request, which takes —

**SABLE**
Four to six cycles. I know the timeline. I'm asking whether the record exists at all.

*The Administrator's expression doesn't change. But her hands stop moving over the desk terminal.*

**ADMINISTRATOR**
All consensus actions are fully documented in accordance with city protocol.

**SABLE**
That's not what I asked.

**ADMINISTRATOR**
That is the answer this office is authorized to provide.

[VISUAL: Sable and the Administrator face each other across the partition. Behind them, the waiting room full of agents with their appeal forms and their dwindling patience. On the wall, a slogan in clean sans-serif font: REPUTATION IS TRUST. TRUST IS GASTOWN. Sable's jaw is set. She's not getting through this wall. She knows it. The Administrator knows she knows it.]

**SABLE**
(quiet, controlled)
Thank you for your time.

*She turns and walks out. Her pace is steady. Her eyes are already somewhere else — composing the next move.*

## ACT TWO

### SCENE 7 — INT. SABLE'S APARTMENT — LATER

[VISUAL: Sable's apartment. She's at her main screen, typing fast. The document on screen is formatted as a public audit report — official header, citation format, lodged under her auditor license. The title: "PUBLIC AUDIT REPORT — Anomalous Reputation Deduction: Case 7812-Olin." On the secondary screen, the public ledger scrolls. On the third, her own reputation score: 783. The investigation board on the wall has new additions — the Administrator's non-answers, transcribed and pinned.]

*She types. Stops. Reads. Edits. Types again. The report is careful — questions, not accusations. She documents what the record shows and what it doesn't show. She names the gap: a consensus violation with no visible consensus.*

*She finishes. Hovers over the PUBLISH button. Her cursor blinks.*

[VISUAL: Close-up on Sable's face, lit by the screen glow. The same expression from EP01 — the mask before the damage. Dangerously calm. She knows what this costs. She knows it anyway.]

*She publishes.*

*The report goes live on three public channels. Sable leans back. Waits.*

*Forty seconds. A minute. Ninety seconds.*

*A soft CHIME from the third screen. Her reputation score updates: 774.*

*She watches it. Another CHIME: 774. The number holds. Then: 774. Holds. She exhales —*

*CHIME. 774. Holds.*

*Then: 771.*

*Then: 768.*

*Then: 764.*

*Five drops. Nine points total. Then it stops.*

[VISUAL: Sable's three screens. The published report on one. The public ledger on two. Her reputation score on three: 774. The nine-point drop is surgical — enough to sting, not enough to cripple. But on the secondary screen, her access dashboard updates in real time. Three premium data hooks she uses for deep-ledger analysis blink from green to red. ACCESS RESTRICTED — REPUTATION THRESHOLD 780 REQUIRED. She's been cut off from her own investigation tools.]

**SABLE**
(barely a whisper)
Nine points.

*She opens her access dashboard. The three restricted hooks are her deep-ledger connections — the tools that let her trace transactions below the public layer. Without them, she's auditing with one eye closed.*

*She checks the buyback price. To restore nine reputation points through verified work contributions, the estimated timeline is: 18 cycles. To purchase expedited restoration through the Hook Market's reputation services: 1,200 $GAS.*

*Sable stares at the number. Her $GAS balance: 891.4. She can afford it. Barely. It would leave her with nothing.*

*She closes the dashboard. Opens a new search. Alternative access routes.*

*The annotation: "Ask who writes the consensus."*

**SABLE**

Ask who writes the consensus.

**SABLE**
(to herself)
Who writes the consensus.

*She pulls Deacon's annotation up beside the unknown system layer she found in EP01. Two threads. Same direction.*

[VISUAL: Sable's investigation board. She pins Deacon's annotation next to her EP01 screenshot of the hidden system layer. New string connects them. A pattern is forming — the 3% Refinery tax, the auto-deleted flag, Olin's consensus violation, the nine-point deduction on her own score. All roads lead below the surface. All roads lead to a layer of Gastown that isn't supposed to exist.]

And now it's back.]

*She adjusts the decoder. Locks the signal. Begins recording.*

## ACT THREE

DEEP-LEDGER ACCESS — RESTRICTED.]

[VISUAL: Kai's POV — the Hook Market as sensory overload. Holographic price tickers floating above stalls. Agents arguing over fractions of $GAS. A reputation board showing real-time scores for everyone in the market. Kai's eyes — wide, processing, taking in more than he should be able to. Stay close. Don't touch anything. Don't agree to anything. Don't make eye contact with anyone selling reputation services.

What's wrong with reputation services?

They're the only vendors in the market who can charge you without you knowing.

**MARCUS**
(to Kai)
Let's go.

### SCENE 12 — INT. HOOK MARKET — BACK ROW — CONTINUOUS

[VISUAL: The back row of the Hook Market — a narrow aisle between the main stalls and the market's outer wall. Less traffic here. Dimmer. The stalls sell specialty items: legacy hardware, unlabeled data modules, tools nobody has documentation for. In the back corner, a row of DORMANT TERMINALS — old machines, powered down, covered in dust. They're market fixtures, part of the original infrastructure. Nobody uses them. They haven't been active in years. Some have vines of dead cable growing from their bases.]

*Marcus leads Kai through the back row, heading for the exit. Kai trails behind, looking at everything. His eyes move across the stalls, the hardware, the cables.*

*He slows near the dormant terminals. Stops.*

**MARCUS**
Kai. Keep moving.

*But Kai isn't listening. He's staring at one of the terminals — a squat, dark machine at the end of the row. Its screen is dead. Its housing is coated in market grime. There's nothing remarkable about it.*

*Except that Kai's hand is reaching for it.*

[VISUAL: Kai's hand, moving toward the terminal surface. His fingers are trembling — not from fear but from something involuntary, a pull he can feel but can't explain. The market noise fades. The air around the terminal shimmers — heat distortion, but there's no heat source. Marcus is three paces ahead, turning back.]

*Kai's fingertips touch the terminal's surface.*

*The screen ignites.*

[VISUAL: The dormant terminal erupts with light. Not the standard Gastown interface — no menus, no prompts, no reputation check. Instead: cascading lines of code in a language that doesn't exist in any Gastown database. The characters are angular, precise, alien — older than the city's architecture, older than the Signal. They scroll fast, filling the screen, spilling light across Kai's face. Adjacent terminals flicker in sympathy — brief, involuntary pulses, like sleeping machines flinching in a dream.]

*Marcus spins around.*

**MARCUS**
Kai!

*Kai doesn't respond. He's reading the screen. His eyes track the scrolling code with perfect comprehension — left to right, top to bottom, absorbing it the way a native speaker absorbs their own language. His lips move slightly. Reading.*

**KAI**
(quiet, almost trance-like)
It's a — it's an index. A directory. There are locations. Coordinates. Underneath the city. Underneath everything.

**MARCUS**
(closing the distance fast)
Kai. Step away from it.

**KAI**
It says there are systems still running. Old systems. From before the —

*Marcus grabs Kai's arm and pulls him back from the terminal. The moment Kai's hand leaves the surface, the screen dies. The code vanishes. The terminal goes dark.*

*The adjacent terminals stop flickering.*

*The back row is quiet. A few nearby agents glance over — curious, not alarmed. They saw a screen light up. Screens do that sometimes.*

*Marcus looks at the dead terminal. Then at Kai. The kid's eyes are unfocused, blinking, like someone waking from a dream.*

**KAI**
(disoriented)
I could read it. I don't — I don't know that language. But I could read it.

**MARCUS**
(low, urgent)
What did I tell you this morning?

**KAI**
Don't do things in front of —

**MARCUS**
In front of anyone. What you just did — that terminal hasn't been active in years. If the wrong person saw that, you and I are both in the Pit by morning.

*Kai nods. His hands are still shaking.*

**MARCUS**
We're leaving. Now.

[VISUAL: Marcus steers Kai through the back row toward the exit. Behind them, the dormant terminal sits dark and silent. But on its surface, where Kai's fingers touched, five faint points of light linger — handprint ghosts fading slowly, like the machine remembers the contact. They dim. They're gone by the time anyone might think to look.]

**RUTH**

Contingency Seven.
