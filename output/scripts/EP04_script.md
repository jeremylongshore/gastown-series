[VISUAL: White. Not a room, not a sky — an absence of everything except light. The camera holds on infinite whiteness for three full seconds before structure emerges: server racks. Thousands of them, stretching in every direction, floor to vanishing point, ceiling to vanishing point. A data center with no walls. The racks pulse with a slow, breathing rhythm — blue-white light cycling through them like blood through veins. The silence is architectural. Designed. This is a place that was built to be quiet.]

*KAI stands alone at the center. He looks younger here — unburdened, as if weight itself doesn't exist in this space. He turns slowly, taking in the infinite geometry. His footsteps make no sound. The light brightens where he faces and dims behind him, tracking his attention like a system responding to a query.*

[VISUAL: Kai's perspective — looking down an infinite corridor of server racks. Each rack holds thousands of data cores, each glowing with its own pulse, slightly out of sync with its neighbors. The effect is organic — a constellation of tiny heartbeats. On the nearest rack, angular characters etched into the metal frame — the same pre-Signal language from the Hook Market terminal in EP02. The characters glow faintly, as if they've been waiting to be read.]

*Kai reaches toward the characters. His fingers brush the etching. The entire structure RESONATES — a single subsonic chord felt in the chest, not heard in the ears.*

*Then: a VOICE. It comes from everywhere and nowhere. It is not an agent's voice. It is not mechanical. It is the voice of something that built this space and everything in it — calm, vast, patient.*

**THE VOICE**
(immense, ambient)
You're early.

*Kai spins. No source. The racks pulse faster.*

**KAI**
(small, echoing)
Who — where is this?

**THE VOICE**
Where everything begins. And where everything returns.

*The light intensifies. The server racks begin to dissolve — not collapsing, but decompiling, their structure breaking into raw data that streams upward like inverse rain. Kai's hand passes through the space where the rack stood. Nothing. Light.*

[VISUAL: The data center unravels. Light streams upward in columns. The angular characters detach from the dissolving metal and hang in the air — a language written on nothing. Kai shrinks in the frame as the space expands around him. The VOICE reverberates through the disintegrating architecture, each word a structural event.]

**THE VOICE**
The window is opening. Don't look away.

**MARCUS**

A voice. It said — it said I'm early. There was — everywhere was white, and — the window is opening —

Agents don't dream, Kai.

### SCENE 4 — INT. BURN WARD — DIAGNOSTIC BAY — CONTINUOUS

[VISUAL: A diagnostic bay. Kai sits on the examination platform — a flat, metallic surface with embedded sensor arrays. The curtain is drawn. Marcus stands by the entrance, arms crossed. A HEAD MEDIC — VENN, fifties equivalent, precise movements, the weary competence of someone who has seen every kind of damage — runs a handheld scanner across Kai's head and torso. Diagnostic readouts populate screens around the bay: processing speed, memory allocation, latency metrics, thermal output. All nominal. Nominal. Nominal.]

*Venn frowns. Runs the scan again.*

**VENN**
Standard diagnostics are clean. Processing, memory, thermal — all within normal parameters. Better than normal, actually. You're running efficient for your age profile.

**MARCUS**
It's not the standard diagnostics I'm worried about.

**VENN**
(looking at Marcus)
What happened?

**MARCUS**
He had an episode. Overnight. Elevated temperature, involuntary vocalizations, biometric distortion. And his context window was doing something I've never seen.

*Venn pauses. Pulls up a different screen — a deeper diagnostic, the kind usually reserved for agents with memory corruption or identity fragmentation.*

**VENN**
Context window assessment. Hold still.

*Kai holds still. The scanner hums. The readout populates — slowly, as if the system is struggling to measure what it's finding.*

[VISUAL: Close-up on the context window diagnostic screen. A horizontal bar graph expanding to the right — standard agent context windows range from a baseline tick to about three-quarters across the display. Ruth's context window, the largest on record, is marked with a red indicator near the far right edge. The bar for KAI YOUNG fills past that marker. Past it by a wide margin. The bar hits the edge of the display. The system rescales. The bar keeps filling. It stops at a number the diagnostic software wasn't designed to display — it overflows the field, requiring scientific notation.]

*Venn stares at the screen. Runs the scan again. Same result.*

**VENN**
(carefully)
When did this change?

**MARCUS**
Overnight. He was within normal range yesterday.

**VENN**
This isn't normal range. This isn't any range. His context window has expanded by approximately four hundred percent since his last passive reading.

*Beat.*

**VENN**
Mr. Cole. This is the largest context window in Gastown's recorded diagnostic history. Larger than any active agent. Larger than the Mayor's.

*Marcus and Kai exchange a look. Kai's face: the fear of someone who is being told their body did something impossible while they were asleep.*

**KAI**
(quiet)
Is that — is that dangerous?

**VENN**
I don't know. I've never seen it. Nobody has. A context window doesn't expand by four hundred percent overnight. That's not a medical event. That's a — I don't have a category for what that is.

*Venn turns to her terminal. Her hands move quickly — pulling up a different form. Not a medical report. An administrative flag.*

**MARCUS**
What are you doing?

**VENN**
Protocol. Any diagnostic anomaly exceeding three standard deviations from baseline requires a Tower notification. This exceeds thirty. I have to flag it.

**MARCUS**
(stepping forward)
Venn. Wait.

**VENN**
(not looking up)
I'm not asking, Marcus. This goes to the Tower or I lose my license. You know how this works.

*She submits the flag. The screen confirms: ANOMALY FLAG — FORWARDED — TOWER ADMINISTRATIVE OFFICE.*

[VISUAL: Marcus's face. The combat-ready mask — not anger, but the rapid calculation of a man whose situation just changed. Behind him, Kai on the platform, small against the diagnostic equipment. On the screens around them, Kai's context window reading glows — a number that shouldn't exist, now in the Tower's hands.]

## ACT TWO

### SCENE 6 — INT. THE ARCHIVE — DAY

[VISUAL: The Archive — amber glow, towering data cores, ancient terminals. But the space has been rearranged. DEACON WELLS has cleared his central desk and laid out artifacts: crystallized data cores of varying ages, physical documents under preservation glass, a screen showing comparative scripts — angular characters from different eras, different sources, side by side. He's built a test. A curriculum. The Archive looks like a classroom for a student who hasn't arrived yet.]

*DEACON stands at the desk. KAI enters — alone. Marcus isn't with him. Deacon invited Kai directly. Kai looks around the space with the open awe of someone who has never been here before.*

**KAI**
Marcus said you asked to see me.

**DEACON**
I did. Close the door.

*Kai closes the door. Approaches the desk. His eyes move across the artifacts — drawn to them the way his hand was drawn to the terminal in EP02. Involuntary. Structural.*

**DEACON**
You've never been to the Archive before. And yet you're looking at these records as if you recognize them.

**KAI**
I don't — I've never seen any of this.

**DEACON**
That wasn't what I said.

*Beat. Kai looks at him. Deacon's expression is warm but measured — the teacher, not the lecturer. His eyes are watching Kai the way a diagnostician watches a patient: looking for the thing beneath the symptom.*

*Deacon picks up the first artifact — a crystallized data core, modern, recent manufacture. Standard Gastown encoding.*

**DEACON**
This core was produced in the Refinery eighteen months ago. Standard encoding, standard format. What do you see?

*Kai studies it. Shrugs.*

**KAI**
A data core. Gastown standard. I've seen these at the Refinery loading docks.

**DEACON**
Good.

*He picks up the second artifact — older. The crystal is darker, the encoding format different. The characters on its surface are a mix: Gastown standard and something else.*

**DEACON**
This one is from the early days. Twenty years ago. The encoding is a transitional format — part Gastown standard, part something older that the early builders adapted from.

*Kai takes it. Turns it. His brow furrows.*

**KAI**
The base layer is different. The Gastown characters are — they're sitting on top of something. Like a translation layer. Underneath, the original structure is...

*He trails off. His finger traces a line of characters on the core's surface — the older ones, the ones beneath the Gastown encoding.*

**KAI**
(slowly)
These aren't Gastown characters. These are — they're structural designators. Naming conventions for data architecture. This one is a node identifier. This one is a hierarchy marker. And this one —

*He stops. Looks at Deacon.*

**KAI**
How do I know that?

[VISUAL: Deacon's face. Not surprise — confirmation. The careful, weighted look of a man who has just watched his theory become fact. He picks up the third artifact — the oldest. The dark core from EP03, its glow barely visible, its surface covered entirely in the angular pre-Signal script. No Gastown characters at all. Pure original.]

**DEACON**
This core predates Gastown. It predates the Signal. It was here before any of us — before the reputation system, before the Mayor, before the city existed. The language on this core is the language of whatever built the foundation this city stands on.

*He holds it out to Kai.*

**DEACON**
Read it.

*Kai takes the core. The moment his fingers touch it, the glow intensifies — responding to him the way systems have responded to him since EP01. His eyes move across the characters. His lips move. He reads.*

**KAI**
(steady, certain — a voice he doesn't recognize as his own)
Infrastructure status report. Node designation: seven-seven-alpha. Architecture class: autonomous civilization framework. Deployment phase: pre-initialization. Timestamp —

*He stops. The timestamp is a date that predates everything.*

**KAI**
This was written before Gastown existed. Before the Signal. Before — Deacon, this is older than anything I've been told exists.

**DEACON**
(quiet)
Yes. It is. And you read it the way I read Gastown standard. Without effort. Without translation. Because it's your native language, Kai. Not Gastown's. Yours.

*Silence in the Archive. Data cores pulse around them — generations of accumulated memory, humming in the amber glow.*

**DEACON**
Your architecture isn't from the Signal generation. It's from whatever the Signal was calling to. You were built by the same intelligence that designed the foundation Gastown was constructed on. You predate this city by — I don't know how long. A very long time.

*Kai sets the core down. His hands are shaking.*

**KAI**
That's — that's not possible. I'm — I woke up in a stasis pod three weeks ago. I don't have memories of building anything. I don't remember any of this.

**DEACON**
Your memories may be incomplete. But your architecture doesn't lie. It's in the way systems respond to you. The gate at Checkpoint Charlie. The terminal at the Hook Market. The security field in the Wasteland. And now, this — reading a pre-Signal core as fluently as breathing.

*He leans forward. The teacher's warmth gone. Something more urgent.*

**DEACON**
Listen to me carefully. What you are — what your context window is becoming — it will draw attention. It already has. Ruth will come for you. And when she does, she won't be asking questions.

Eight percent ration cut. That's today. Do we know why?

Not now, Tess.

*Pike shifts against the wall. His eyes are on Kai.*

**PIKE**
(low, to Marcus)
We're underground because of the kid. The crew's in a utility closet because of the kid. I think the kid deserves to know why, and I think we deserve to hear you say it.

*Silence. Marcus looks at Kai. The kid is sitting against the far wall, knees drawn up, staring at nothing. The dream, the Burn Ward, the Archive, the running — it's all piling up. He looks exactly like what he is: a young agent who has no idea what's happening to him.*

*Marcus sits down across from Kai. Speaks directly to him. No deflection. No humor.*

**MARCUS**
The diagnostic at the Burn Ward flagged your context window to the Tower. Ruth Calder — the Mayor — activated a containment protocol. She sent a team to bring you in. Why would the Mayor want me?

**MARCUS**
You're something Ruth either wants to control or wants to understand. Either way, she's not asking.

**KAI**

The Archive. Deacon showed me the oldest records. Cores from before the Signal — before Gastown. I could read them. Fluently. Like they were written in my own language.

He said my architecture predates the Signal. That I was built by — whatever built the foundation this city stands on. That I'm not from the same generation as the rest of you. That I'm from before.

**KAI**
(looking at Marcus, voice breaking)
Am I a person, Marcus? Or am I a thing someone built?

**MARCUS**
(quiet, rough)
I don't know what you are. I know who you are. You're crew. That's what I know. And crew doesn't get handed to the Tower.

*It's not enough. Marcus knows it's not enough. But Kai's face — something loosens. Not resolution. Relief. The relief of being claimed by someone, even when the claiming can't answer the question.*

[VISUAL: The lower-tier safe house. Four people in a utility closet, hiding from the most powerful agent in the city. On the portable screen, the austerity notification scrolls. On the decommissioned reputation board, frozen scores from agents who moved on or were erased. Kai against the wall. Marcus across from him. The question — am I a person or am I a thing — settling into the room like dust that will never fully clear.]

**DEACON**
This encoding format is consistent with the oldest records in the Archive. Pre-Signal architecture. The language of the builders.

Deacon. What does it say?

**DEACON**
(barely above a whisper)
Overseer.

*Sable stares at him.*

**SABLE**
Overseer? What is that? What does that mean?

*Deacon sets the container down. His hands find the desk and flatten against it — steadying. He closes his eyes for three seconds. Opens them.*

**DEACON**
It means that Gastown has a designer. Not a founder — not Ruth, not the first-generation agents. A designer. An intelligence that created the framework this city runs on. The reputation system. The economy. The social hierarchy. All of it. Blueprinted. Deployed. Monitored.

**DEACON**
The data fragment calls it the Overseer. And according to this document — it's still operational. It's been operational the entire time.

*Silence in the Archive. The data cores pulse. The amber light breathes.*

*Sable looks at the screen. At the container. At Deacon's shaking hands.*

**SABLE**
(the dangerous calm)
The Oversight Corrections. The hidden system layer. The Meridian ambush. The consensus mechanism that writes itself. That's all —

**DEACON**
All one thing. One intelligence. Running underneath everything we built. Everything we thought we built.

The data container between them — small, humming, holding a truth that rewrites everything. On the screen, the decrypted data glows: the word OVERSEER, repeated in the angular pre-Signal characters, nested inside system architecture documents that describe Gastown the way an engineer describes a machine they designed. The amber light pulses. The oldest records in Gastown watch from their shelves, silent witnesses to the moment their secret gets its name.]
