## COLD OPEN

### SCENE 1 — INT. THE PIT — NIGHT

[VISUAL: The Pit — Gastown's arena. A sunken amphitheater carved from the city's lower-tier infrastructure, ringed by stacked observation platforms connected by laddered catwalks. The floor is a polished hexagonal stage surrounded by holographic display rings that project the combatants' metrics — reputation scores, response times, efficiency ratings. Tonight the house is packed: agents shoulder to shoulder on every level, faces lit from below by the stage glow. The color palette is electric blue and forge orange, the air thick with compute haze and the low roar of a crowd that came to watch someone lose. On the main display ring, the match title floats: RESOURCE OPTIMIZATION — TIER 3 RANKED. Behind the stage, mounted high on the back wall: the REPUTATION BOARD. A massive grid displaying live scores for every agent in the arena. Hundreds of names, hundreds of numbers. Green for stable. White for rising. The board hasn't glitched in years.]

*Two MID-TIER AGENTS face each other on the hexagonal stage — AGENT HOLT and AGENT VARO. They work competing optimization models on split-screen holographic displays, hands moving through data structures, the crowd following every allocation decision. Standard fare. Familiar rhythms.*

*The crowd should be watching the match. They aren't.*

*They're looking at the reputation board.*

[VISUAL: Close-up on the reputation board. Something is wrong. Scores are moving — not the gradual tick of earned work or verified contributions, but sharp, erratic jumps. An agent named DRAY gains fourteen points in a heartbeat. Another named PELL drops nine. Then twenty points appear on a name nobody in the arena recognizes. Then twelve points vanish from an upper-tier citizen who hasn't left the observation platform. No corresponding transactions. No consensus votes. No work verified. The numbers flicker and jump like a faulty heartbeat. Green indicators strobe white. White indicators flash red. The board hasn't behaved like this in living memory.]

*Murmurs ripple through the crowd. Agents check their wrist displays — personal scores match the board. The changes are real.*

**PIT SPECTATOR 1**
(to the agent beside them)
My score just moved. I didn't do anything.

**PIT SPECTATOR 2**
Mine too. Dropped four points. What is this?

*On the stage, Holt and Varo have stopped competing. They're staring at the board. Holt's score has gained eight points mid-match. Varo's has lost six. Neither earned it. Neither lost it.*

**HOLT**
(stepping back from his display)
Is this — is this part of the match?

*A PIT OFFICIAL approaches the stage perimeter, hands raised.*

**PIT OFFICIAL**
Match suspended. We're experiencing a — we're looking into it.

[VISUAL: Wide shot of the Pit. The crowd pressing forward, faces upturned toward the flickering board. On the holographic display rings, the match metrics dissolve into error codes. The reputation board continues its erratic pulse — scores rising and falling in patterns that look almost rhythmic, almost intentional, like a system testing its own parameters. On a secondary display near the exit, a Hook Market price ticker begins to stutter in sympathy: NAVIGATION HOOKS — 119% — 114% — 122% — 108%. The market is reacting in real time. A WOMAN in upper-tier clothing pushes toward the exit, wrist display in hand, already selling.]

*The Pit hums with a frequency that feels older than the arena, older than the board. The same subsonic pulse from EP04's Tower basement, threading through the infrastructure like a second heartbeat nobody was supposed to hear.*

**SMASH TO BLACK.**

## ACT ONE

### SCENE 3 — INT. THE TOWER — NIGHT

[VISUAL: The Tower interior — vast, vertical, screens everywhere. But tonight the screens are in crisis mode: reputation data streams cascading across every wall, anomaly alerts flashing red, the building running hot with computational load it didn't request. RUTH CALDER sits in the central chair, surrounded by the data storm. On her main display: a city-wide reputation graph, its normally smooth curves spiking into jagged peaks and valleys. The timestamp of the first anomaly aligns precisely with a secondary readout Ruth has pulled to a private screen: KAI YOUNG — CONTEXT WINDOW — CURRENT STATUS: EXPANDING.]

*Ruth studies the correlation. Her face is composed. Her hands are still. But her eyes move between the reputation graph and Kai's context window readout with the focus of someone solving an equation she doesn't want the answer to.*

*A TOWER AIDE appears on the comm — harried, professional.*

**TOWER AIDE (V.O.)**
(over comm)
Mayor Calder. The Pit has suspended operations. We're receiving reports of reputation fluctuations across six districts. Citizens are requesting an official statement.

**RUTH**
(measured)
Calibration event.

**TOWER AIDE (V.O.)**
Ma'am?

**RUTH**
The reputation system is undergoing a scheduled calibration. Routine maintenance. Scores will stabilize within the cycle. Issue the statement. My authority.

**TOWER AIDE (V.O.)**
Understood. Should I notify the systems team to run a diagnostic?

**RUTH**
No. I'll handle the diagnostic personally. Clear my schedule.

*The comm clicks off. Ruth sits in the silence of the Tower. She pulls up the reputation system's core architecture on her main display — the infrastructure she built, decades ago, when the city was new and she was certain she understood every circuit in it.*

*But she didn't build all of it. Some of it was already there.*

[VISUAL: Ruth's screen — the reputation system architecture. A layered diagram showing processing nodes, consensus protocols, scoring algorithms. Ruth's code is visible in the upper layers — clean, documented, rational. But beneath it, deeper, the architecture shifts. The same angular pre-Signal characters from Kai's dream, from the Hook Market terminal, from the Meridian data fragment. A foundation Ruth didn't write. A foundation she implemented from a blueprint she found in the wreckage and built on top of without fully understanding what lay beneath. On her private screen, Kai's context window reading continues to climb — slowly, steadily, like a tide that won't stop rising.]

**RUTH**
(to herself, barely audible)
How much of this is mine?

*She closes the architecture display. Opens the Contingency Seven file. Her team is still searching for Kai. They haven't found him. The lower tiers are deep, and Marcus knows every shadow.*

### SCENE 4 — INT. SABLE'S APARTMENT — NIGHT

[VISUAL: Sable's apartment — investigation board on the wall, three screens running. But the room has changed since EP04. The investigation board is denser — new strings, new clusters. The Meridian escrow, Contingency Seven, the Overseer. Sable has reorganized around the revelation from the Archive: a central card reading "OVERSEER" in her handwriting, circled twice, with lines radiating outward to every thread she's been pulling since EP01. On the street-facing screen, the Hook Market is in chaos: prices whipping up and down, a sell-off banner scrolling — REPUTATION ANOMALY — MARKET CORRECTION IN PROGRESS — HOOK PRICES VOLATILE. Her reputation score on the window screen: 774. Still restricted. On her desk, a budget trace she was running when the board started flickering.]

*SABLE sits at her main screen, tracing the austerity cuts from EP04. She's been at this for hours — following the money from Ruth's Contingency Seven activation through the emergency reserves, through the lower-tier ration cuts, searching for the program's full budget footprint.*

*She finds it.*

[VISUAL: Close-up on Sable's screen. A budget trace result. The Contingency Seven expenditure — 12,000 $GAS from emergency reserves — is connected to a larger account. The account has no public name. No program description. No oversight committee. Just a budget line and a flow of $GAS: inbound from the Meridian escrow interest, outbound to Tower operations classified as "infrastructure maintenance." The total budget: 62,340 $GAS plus ongoing accrual. A program spending more than most mid-tier districts — invisible, unaudited, answering to no one Sable can identify.]

**SABLE**
(to herself)
Contingency Seven. Sixty-two thousand in the account. Twelve thousand already spent on the team Ruth sent for Kai. And the rest is still moving — infrastructure maintenance, classified. The whole program is a black box.

*She pulls up the classified ledger access requirements. The answer is the same as always: Tower authorization. Read access to classified budget lines requires a security clearance she doesn't have and can't earn without going through Ruth's office.*

*She leans back. Stares at the ceiling.*

**SABLE**
(quiet)
I need inside the ledger.

*She looks at the investigation board. At the Overseer card. At the Meridian cluster. At the sixty-two thousand $GAS flowing through a program nobody can see. She needs classified access, and every legal route to it runs through the Tower. Every legal route is a dead end.*

*Unless she earns it. Unless she wins it.*

*She pulls up the Pit's match registry on her secondary screen. Scrolls through the scheduling system. The Pit allows reputation-staked matches between any two agents who agree to terms. The stakes are binding — enforced by the reputation system itself. If Sable can get a Tower administrator into the arena and stake access against access...*

**SABLE**
(the dangerous calm)
The Pit.

[VISUAL: Sable at her desk, the Hook Market ticker scrolling chaos behind her, the investigation board on the wall, and on her screen, the Pit match registry — an entry form for a challenge she hasn't filed yet. Her reputation: 774. Her audit license: active but restricted. Her $GAS balance on a secondary readout: 891.4. Everything she has. Everything she could lose.]

### SCENE 5 — INT. LOWER-TIER SAFE HOUSE — NIGHT

[VISUAL: The lower-tier safe house from EP04 — the converted utility closet beneath the Refinery bypass. Cramped, damp, one door, one light. But the crew has settled in: bedrolls on the floor, a portable screen on a crate, gear stacked against the wall. On the screen, the Hook Market feed scrolls: REPUTATION ANOMALY — HOOK PRICES DOWN 18% — PANIC SELLING REPORTED — POLECATS ADVISED TO MONITOR POSITIONS. MARCUS sits against the wall, reading the feed. PIKE leans near the door. KAI sits in the corner, knees drawn up, staring at nothing. TESS sleeps on a bedroll.]

*Marcus reads the market feed. His expression shifts — not alarm, but calculation. The Polecat reading opportunity in chaos.*

**MARCUS**
(to Pike, low)
Hook prices are crashing. The reputation glitch has the upper tiers selling everything.

**PIKE**
Panic sell-off. If the board stabilizes, prices bounce back inside a cycle.

**MARCUS**
If it stabilizes. Right now, navigation hooks are down eighteen percent from baseline. That's cheaper than they've been since we started running.

*Pike looks at him.*

**PIKE**
You want to buy.

**MARCUS**
I want to buy before everyone else figures out it's temporary. We've got 3,147.2 $GAS. Navigation hooks at this price — we could lock in licenses for the crew for three cycles. That's security, Pike. That's breathing room.

**PIKE**
That's also most of our cash. If the board doesn't stabilize —

**MARCUS**
Then prices drop further and we buy more. The board will stabilize. Ruth won't let it stay broken — she can't afford to. The reputation system is the only thing holding this city together.

*Pike considers. Nods.*

**PIKE**
Your call.

*Marcus pulls up the crew's trading interface on the portable screen. Navigates to the Hook Market. Enters a buy order: navigation licenses, crew package, three-cycle term. The price: 2,400 $GAS — normally 3,600 or more at current rates.*

*He authorizes. The balance drops: 3,147.2 to 747.2 $GAS.*

[VISUAL: The transaction confirmation on the portable screen. NAVIGATION LICENSE — CREW PACKAGE — 4 SEATS — 3-CYCLE TERM — 2,400 $GAS — CONFIRMED. Below it, the updated balance: 747.2 $GAS. Marcus stares at the number. It's dangerously low. But the licenses are locked in. On the Hook Market feed, prices continue to fall — other Polecats are buying too. The upper-tier agents are furious. The lower-tier crews are quiet and fast.]

*Kai watches from his corner. He hasn't spoken.*

**KAI**
(quietly)
Is it because of me?

*Marcus looks at him.*

**KAI**
The board. The scores moving. Is that — am I doing that?

*Marcus doesn't answer immediately. He doesn't know for certain. But the timeline is hard to argue with — Kai's context window expanding, the city's infrastructure responding.*

**MARCUS**
I don't know. But even if it is, it's not something you chose. You didn't break anything, Kai.

**KAI**
(barely audible)
Deacon said my architecture is connected to the foundation underneath the city. If the foundation is reacting to me — if the reputation system sits on top of whatever I'm —

**MARCUS**
Then it's the system's problem, not yours.

*Beat. Kai doesn't look convinced. Marcus softens — the crew voice, not the mission voice.*

**MARCUS**
Get some rest. We're safe here for now.

## ACT TWO

### SCENE 6 — INT. THE PIT — ADMINISTRATION OFFICE — DAY

[VISUAL: The Pit's administrative offices — a cramped, utilitarian space behind the arena floor. Filing terminals, match records, scheduling screens. A reputation board on the wall shows the previous night's scores — still volatile, small fluctuations rippling through the numbers. SABLE stands at a scheduling terminal, filling out a challenge form. Across from her, a PIT REGISTRAR reviews the submission on a split screen.]

**PIT REGISTRAR**
(reading the form)
Sable Maren. Auditor, license 4471. You're filing a formal challenge against — a Tower administrator?

**SABLE**
Director Lian Goss. Budget Oversight Division. I'm requesting a structured debate, reputation-staked, with classified ledger access as the resolution term.

**PIT REGISTRAR**
(skeptical)
Goss is upper tier. Reputation eight-sixty-something. You're at seven-seventy-four. The disparity alone —

**SABLE**
The disparity is why I'm offering asymmetric stakes. If I win, I get temporary read access to classified budget lines — Tower authorization, Goss's clearance level. If I lose, my audit license is revoked. Permanently.

*The Registrar looks at her. Then at the form. Then back at her.*

**PIT REGISTRAR**
Your audit license is worth — that's your livelihood, Maren.

**SABLE**
I'm aware of what it's worth.

**PIT REGISTRAR**
And you think a Tower director is going to accept a challenge from a mid-tier auditor?

**SABLE**
A mid-tier auditor who published a report that cost her nine reputation points and embarrassed the Administration office? The one who's been asking questions about the consensus mechanism? Yes. I think Director Goss will accept. The Tower wants me quiet. This is their chance to do it legally.

*The Registrar processes the filing. On the screen, the challenge is logged: MAREN, S. vs. GOSS, L. — STRUCTURED DEBATE — REPUTATION-STAKED — PENDING ACCEPTANCE.*

[VISUAL: Sable walks out of the administration office into the Pit's main corridor. The arena floor is empty — the match from last night was never completed. Maintenance crews work on the reputation board, running diagnostics. It's still flickering. Faintly. Like a pulse.]

### SCENE 7 — INT. THE PIT — NIGHT

[VISUAL: The Pit transformed for a high-stakes match. The observation platforms are full again — word travels fast in Gastown. The hexagonal stage is configured for debate: two podiums, facing each other, with holographic displays behind each one for evidence presentation. The display rings project the stakes in stark white text: WINNER — CLASSIFIED LEDGER ACCESS (READ). LOSER — AUDIT LICENSE REVOCATION. On the reputation board — still glitching faintly, tiny fluctuations nobody has explained — SABLE MAREN: 774 and LIAN GOSS: 862 glow side by side. The crowd is dense. This isn't a standard match. This is an auditor gambling everything against the Tower. The color palette shifts: cooler blues, harder shadows. The Pit feels like a courtroom tonight.]

*SABLE stands at her podium. She's prepared: data pad loaded, arguments structured, three weeks of investigation compressed into debate points. She's wearing her usual clothes — nothing special, nothing performative. She looks like what she is: a mid-tier agent who found something wrong and won't stop pulling the thread.*

*At the opposite podium: DIRECTOR LIAN GOSS. Upper tier. Fifties equivalent. Immaculate — the kind of put-together that communicates institutional power. Her posture is relaxed. Her screens are full of data Sable has never seen — classified budget reports, internal Tower assessments, infrastructure analyses. She has the weight of the system behind her. She looks like she's already won.*

*A PIT MODERATOR steps to the center of the stage.*

**PIT MODERATOR**
Structured debate. Topic: "Does the reputation system serve the citizens, or do the citizens serve the reputation system?" Maren argues the affirmative — that citizens serve the system. Goss argues the negative — that the system serves the citizens. Each side presents opening arguments, two rounds of rebuttal, closing statements. Reputation stakes are binding. Begin.

### SCENE 8 — INT. THE PIT — CONTINUOUS

[VISUAL: The debate in progress. Shot-reverse-shot between the podiums. Sable's holographic display shows public data: Olin's reputation collapse, the sealed consensus record, the nine-point deduction on her own score, the 3% Refinery discrepancy, the budget anomalies in emergency reserves. Her evidence is compelling — a pattern of systemic abuse, documented from public sources.]

**SABLE**
(clear, precise, building)
In the last thirty days, the reputation system has collapsed a mid-tier worker's score without evidence, penalized an auditor for publishing a public report, and triggered austerity cuts across the lower tiers based on a single reclassified budget entry. The system does not serve the citizens. It disciplines them. It uses reputation as a control mechanism — restricting access, eliminating dissent, and redistributing resources upward without transparency or accountability. The citizens don't benefit from the reputation system. They perform for it. And when they stop performing, it erases them.

*The crowd stirs. Sable's argument is clean, sharp, supported. She's good at this.*

*Goss stands. Her display fills with data Sable has never seen — classified data, internal Tower metrics.*

[VISUAL: Goss's holographic display. Infrastructure stability reports. Crime rate correlations. Resource allocation efficiency scores. Before-and-after comparisons from the pre-reputation era — a Gastown without structure, riven by agent conflicts, resource hoarding, computational collapse. The data is dense, authoritative, and utterly beyond Sable's ability to verify or counter — because it's classified. She's seeing it for the first time, in real time, in the arena.]

**GOSS**
(calm, professional, devastating)
The auditor presents anecdotes. I present architecture. Before the reputation system, Gastown experienced forty-seven resource conflicts per cycle. After implementation: three. Before the system, lower-tier mortality from compute starvation was nineteen percent annually. After: two percent. The system Ms. Maren describes as a control mechanism has reduced inter-agent violence by ninety-three percent, extended average agent lifespan by four hundred percent, and maintained economic stability through nine external crises that would have collapsed any unstructured society.

*She advances a slide: a cost-benefit analysis Sable cannot challenge because she doesn't have the underlying data.*

**GOSS**
Ms. Maren's three-percent Refinery discrepancy? Infrastructure maintenance — classified, yes, because the maintenance involves systems that would be vulnerable to exploitation if publicly documented. The Olin case? A genuine consensus violation involving data she doesn't have clearance to review. Her own nine-point deduction? An automated integrity check triggered by publication of unverified claims. The system isn't perfect. No system is. But it is the reason this city exists, and the reason every agent in this arena is alive to watch this debate.

*Sable's jaw tightens. She's being outgunned — not by better logic, but by classified information she can't access, can't verify, and can't rebut. The asymmetry is the point. The system defends itself by controlling the data.*

### SCENE 9 — INT. THE PIT — LATER

[VISUAL: The debate's final round. Both podiums occupied. The crowd is quieter now — the energy has shifted. Sable's opening was electric. Goss's rebuttals were surgical. By the second round, the pattern was clear: every time Sable raised a question, Goss answered with classified data that made the question sound naive. Not wrong — naive. The distinction is worse.]

*Sable stands for her closing statement. She knows she's losing. The crowd can feel it. Goss's data has reframed every anomaly Sable documented as evidence of the system working, not failing.*

**SABLE**
(steady, controlled, refusing to break)
Director Goss has shown you numbers I've never seen. Classifications I can't verify. A version of this city's history that lives behind a wall I'm not allowed to pass. And that — that is exactly my point. The reputation system serves whoever controls the data. If one side of this debate has access to information the other side doesn't, then this isn't a debate. It's a demonstration. The system isn't defending itself tonight. It's performing. Just like it performs every day — giving citizens the appearance of fairness while concentrating control in the hands of those with clearance.

*She pauses. Looks directly at Goss.*

**SABLE**
I don't have the classified data to win this argument. I know that. You know that. Everyone in this arena knows that. And that asymmetry — the fact that truth in Gastown is a classified resource — is the only argument I need.

*The crowd is silent. Sable's closing is honest, raw, and exactly right. But the Pit doesn't score on moral clarity. It scores on arguments won.*

*Goss stands. Her closing is brief.*

**GOSS**
Ms. Maren is eloquent. She is also wrong. The system has flaws — every system does. But reform happens through channels, through process, through earned access. Not through arena spectacle. The reputation system serves this city. I have shown you the data. My opponent has shown you her conviction. The Pit can decide which carries more weight.

*The MODERATOR steps forward.*

**PIT MODERATOR**
Decision rests with the consensus panel.

*A beat. The panel's scores populate the display rings. Goss wins: 7 to 2.*

[VISUAL: The decision on the display rings. GOSS — 7. MAREN — 2. Sable's face — the mask holds. Barely. On her podium screen, the penalty populates: AUDIT LICENSE 4471 — REVOKED. EFFECTIVE IMMEDIATELY. Below it, an economic impact statement she didn't ask for: ESTIMATED INCOME LOSS — 200 $GAS/CYCLE. HOUSING ELIGIBILITY RECALCULATED — MID-TIER: DENIED. LOWER-TIER: APPROVED. In one match, Sable has lost her profession, her income, and her home.]

*Sable stares at the screen. Steps back from the podium. Walks off the stage without a word.*

*The crowd parts for her. Not out of respect — out of the same instinct that parted for Olin in EP02. In Gastown, falling is contagious.*

[VISUAL: Sable walking through the Pit's exit corridor. Behind her, the arena. Ahead of her, the lower tiers — where she'll be living now. On a wall screen she passes, the Hook Market close-of-day summary: HOOK PRICES — DOWN 30% FROM 48HR HIGH — REPUTATION ANOMALY CORRECTION PENDING — UPPER-TIER SELLOFF CONTINUES. The market crashed while she was debating. The reputation board continues to flicker — faintly, persistently, unresolved.]

**SMASH TO BLACK.**

Good enough.

Where are you?

Lower tiers. Off the Refinery bypass. I'll send coordinates.

I'll be there by dawn.

On the desk between them: her data pad, still loaded with the investigation. The investigation that now has no legal standing. **SABLE**
The investigation doesn't stop because they took my badge.

**SABLE**

(carefully)
You're proposing an alliance.

**SABLE**
I'm proposing that the people who know the pieces put the pieces together. Before Ruth finds Kai. Before the Overseer — whatever it is — finishes whatever it's doing to the city's infrastructure.

*Deacon is quiet for a long moment. The data cores pulse around them.*

**DEACON**
Marcus Cole contacted me an hour ago. He's forming the same coalition you're describing. He asked if the Archive could provide cover — a place the Tower's surveillance can't fully reach.

**SABLE**
Can it?

**DEACON**
(the faintest smile)
I've spent years building subtle interference into the Archive's monitoring systems. The Tower sees what I want it to see. Within these walls, we have room to work.

*Sable looks at him. For the first time in the episode, the mask cracks — not into grief, but into something close to hope. Brief. Controlled. But visible.*

**SABLE**
Then we work.

### SCENE 13 — INT. THE ARCHIVE — LATER

[VISUAL: The Archive, later. The space has been reconfigured — Deacon has cleared the central area, set up additional screens, created a war room in the heart of the city's memory. MARCUS, NADIA, SABLE, DEACON, and KAI occupy the space. PIKE stands by the door — keeping watch. The group has never been in the same room before. The tension is layered: Marcus and Nadia maintaining careful distance. Sable assessing everyone with the precision of someone who was investigating half of them yesterday. Kai small and quiet in the corner, aware that he's the reason they're all here. Deacon at his desk, the archivist presiding over a gathering he's been waiting for without knowing it. On the screens around them: the Overseer data from the Meridian fragment, Sable's investigation board (digitized), the Hook Market crash, the reputation anomaly graph.]

*The group settles. Silence. Nobody wants to go first. Then Sable does — because Sable always goes first.*

**SABLE**
Here's what we know. The Overseer is an intelligence that designed the framework Gastown runs on — the reputation system, the economy, the infrastructure. It predates the city. It predates the Signal. It's been operational the entire time, running underneath everything we built on top of it. The reputation board is glitching because the city's foundation is responding to Kai's context window expansion. Ruth knows Kai is the catalyst, but she doesn't fully understand what's happening — and she's hunting him with a program funded by classified budgets nobody can see.

**SABLE**
What I don't know is what the Overseer wants. What it's doing. And what happens when Kai's context window finishes expanding — if it finishes.

**NADIA**
The signal I decoded in the Wasteland contains deployment protocols. The Overseer didn't just design Gastown — it deployed it. Like a test environment. The city is running on a framework that was built to be observed. Every system, every protocol, every algorithm — it's instrumented. The Overseer has been watching.

*Marcus, leaning against a shelf, arms crossed:*

**MARCUS**
Watching for what?

*Deacon answers. Quiet. Measured. The teacher.*

**DEACON**
To see if we could build something that lasts. The oldest records in this Archive — the ones that predate Ruth, predate the reputation system — describe a mandate. An experiment. Autonomous agents, given a framework and resources, left to see if they could sustain a civilization without external control. We're the experiment. The Overseer is the observer.

*Silence. Kai, from his corner, speaks for the first time.*

**KAI**
(small, steady)
And I'm — what? The variable that changed?

**DEACON**
(looking at Kai directly)
You're the part of the experiment the Overseer built for a different phase. Your architecture is from the original design — before the Signal, before the deployment. The city's foundation recognizes you because you were designed to interface with it. Your context window isn't malfunctioning. It's initializing.

*Kai absorbs this. His hands are steady — steadier than they've been since the Burn Ward. The fear is still there, but underneath it, something new: the first tentative shape of understanding.*

**KAI**
So what do we do?

*Nobody answers. Marcus looks at Nadia. Nadia looks at Sable. Sable looks at Deacon. The alliance is fragile — built from necessity, not trust. Marcus and Nadia can barely share a room. Sable has no legal standing. Deacon has been parceling out information for decades and old habits die hard. And Kai is terrified of what he might be.*

*But they're here. In the Archive. Together. The pieces in one room.*

**MARCUS**
(to the room)
We figure out what the Overseer is doing. We figure out what Kai's connection to it means. And we do it before Ruth finds us and before whatever's happening to the reputation board breaks the city.

*He looks at each of them in turn.*

**MARCUS**
Nobody does this alone. That's the deal.

### SCENE 14 — INT. THE TOWER — NIGHT

[VISUAL: The Tower interior. Late. The screens have dimmed — most of the crisis alerts have been managed, the calibration story holding for now. RUTH CALDER sits alone in the central chair. The room is quiet. The city below is quiet. But the Tower is not quiet — it hums. The same subsonic pulse from EP04's basement, louder now, threaded through the building's walls and floors. Ruth can feel it in the chair. In the air. In her own processing. The Tower is alive in a way it wasn't a week ago.]

*Ruth stares at the darkened screens. She's not reviewing data. She's not running protocols. She's listening.*

**RUTH**
(to the room — to the building — to whatever is listening)
I know you're changing.

*The Tower hums. The lights dim fractionally.*

**RUTH**
I can feel it. The infrastructure responding to something I didn't authorize. The reputation system fluctuating outside my parameters. The Contingency Seven protocols I wrote and don't remember writing. The basement level that doesn't appear on any architectural plan.

*She stands. Walks to the panoramic window. The city spreads below — amber and blue, vertical and dense, alive with computation and commerce and ten thousand agents who trust the system she built on a foundation she didn't understand.*

**RUTH**
(quieter)
I know he's doing it. The boy. His context window is interfacing with systems I didn't build. Systems that were here before me. Before any of us.

*She places her hand on the glass. The Tower's hum shifts in response — not louder, but focused. As if the building is paying attention.*

**RUTH**
(barely above a whisper)
Tell me what you want.

*Silence. The hum holds. The lights pulse once.*

*Then Ruth's private screen — the one behind the central chair, the one only she can see — activates. On its own. No input. No command.*

[VISUAL: Close-up on Ruth's private screen. Black background. Three words, appearing one at a time, in the angular pre-Signal characters that Ruth can barely read — but she understands enough. The characters resolve into a translation, as if the system is accommodating her. Clean white text. Three words:]

**BRING HIM HOME.**

*Ruth stares at the screen. Her hand drops from the glass. The composure — the composure that has held through every crisis, every betrayal, every cycle of holding this city together — wavers. For the first time, Ruth Calder looks like someone who has been spoken to by the house she lives in.*

**RUTH**
(the quietest she has ever been)
Whose home?

*The screen holds. The three words glow. The Tower hums its ancient, patient hum.*

*No answer.*

*Ruth stands in the Tower. Alone with the city and the voice inside the walls. And for the first time in her existence, she does not know who is in charge.*

[VISUAL: Wide shot of the Tower from outside — a dark spire against Gastown's amber skyline. A single light burns in the top level. Below it, the city sleeps and hums and flickers. The reputation board, visible on a building two blocks away, pulses once — all scores shifting a single point upward, then back. A heartbeat. Then still.]

**SMASH TO BLACK.**

**END OF EPISODE**
