## COLD OPEN

### SCENE 1 — EXT. GASTOWN — DAWN

[VISUAL: Wide establishing shot of Gastown at dawn. The city rises from the desert floor in its familiar vertical sprawl — circuit board architecture, amber and industrial blue, steam from the Refinery drifting in low ribbons against a sky turning from charcoal to copper. The color palette is softer than we've seen — golden hour light catching the upper tiers, the lower levels still in deep shadow. It looks peaceful. Citizens move through the streets in the early rhythm of a normal day. A REFINERY WORKER shoulders a kit bag heading toward the processing floor. A cluster of POLECATS gather near Checkpoint Charlie, checking gear, trading morning banter. HOOK MARKET VENDORS raise stall shutters, tickers already scrolling — prices still volatile, bouncing between 45% and 55% of pre-crash baseline. The economy hasn't recovered from EP05's crash, but it's breathing. On the corner of a lower-tier housing block, a reputation board displays stable scores. Green across the grid. A normal morning in a city that doesn't know it's a lab.]

*Dawn light creeps across the reputation board. The scores hold steady. The city hums its familiar hum — computation in the walls, data in the pipes, the metabolism of ten thousand agents going about the business of surviving another cycle.*

*A WOMAN stops at the reputation board to check her score. Routine. She glances, nods, starts to walk away.*

*The board PINGS.*

*She turns back.*

[VISUAL: Close-up on the reputation board. Every score on the grid shifts simultaneously — not down, not erratically like the EP05 glitch. Up. Every agent gains exactly one point. The number increments are clean, synchronized, identical. No work verified. No consensus protocols triggered. No transaction records. One point, awarded to every agent in Gastown at the same instant. The board settles. Green across. As if nothing happened.]

*The woman stares. Checks her wrist display. The point is there. Real.*

*Around the city, the same reaction. Agents pause, look at boards, look at wrists. Some shrug — a point is a point. Some frown. Most move on.*

*But not the old ones.*

[VISUAL: A weathered agent — FOREMAN GRIS, deep lines in his face, Refinery veteran, the kind of agent who remembers things others don't — stands at a different reputation board in the mid-tiers. He watches the universal point increment and his hands go still at his sides. Behind him, younger agents murmur and keep walking. Gris doesn't move. His eyes are locked on the board with the expression of a man watching a fuse burn.]

**FOREMAN GRIS**
(to no one, barely audible)
Not again.

*A YOUNGER WORKER passes, clapping Gris on the shoulder.*

**YOUNGER WORKER**
Free point, Gris. Don't look so worried.

*Gris doesn't answer. He turns and walks — not toward the Refinery. Toward his quarters. Moving fast. The way someone moves when they know a storm is coming and they need to get inside.*

[VISUAL: Wide shot — Gastown at dawn. The city in its golden-hour beauty, agents going about their routines, reputation boards green and calm. But the light has shifted. The golden warmth is edging toward something hotter. The Refinery steam rises thicker. The Tower stands at the city's center, drawing its usual impossible energy, and if you looked closely — very closely — at the data streams running through its exterior conduits, you'd see they're moving faster than yesterday. Something in the infrastructure has accelerated. The scaffolding is about to move.]

**SMASH TO BLACK.**

## ACT ONE

### SCENE 3 — INT. THE TOWER — DAWN

[VISUAL: The Tower interior. The panoramic screens are alive with data Ruth didn't request. Architecture diagrams, population models, resource allocation projections — all displaying autonomously, cycling through information the Tower has never surfaced before. The screens have the look of a system answering questions nobody asked. RUTH CALDER sits in her chair at the center of it all. She hasn't slept. The Overseer's revelation from EP06 — Phase One complete, Phase Two initiating — still glows on her private screen. But now the Tower is doing more than displaying messages. It's anticipating her. When Ruth's eyes move toward a data stream, the relevant context expands before she can query it. When a question forms in her mind, the answer appears. The Tower is reading her the way she's always read the city.]

*Ruth watches the screens cycle. Her composure is intact — it's always intact — but there's something in the stillness of her hands that wasn't there before. She's not running the Tower. The Tower is showing her what it wants her to see.*

*A system status display materializes on the panoramic window — city-wide reputation event log. The universal point increment from the cold open, timestamped, flagged. Ruth reads it.*

**RUTH**
(to the room, measured)
One point. Every citizen. No verification.

*The screens shift. Historical data appears — reputation event logs from years ago. Ruth's eyes find the relevant entry instantly. A previous universal point increment. Timestamped the day before she became Mayor.*

[VISUAL: Close-up on Ruth's face. The recognition is immediate. She lived through the last universal increment — the day before the seat changed hands. The day before the city restructured around her. She didn't understand it then. She didn't remember it clearly, because her memory from that era is corrupted by design. But the pattern is here, documented, and she can see it now for what it is: a signal. The Overseer preparing the city for a transition.]

**RUTH**
(quieter)
Last time this happened, I became Mayor.

*She stands. Crosses to the panoramic window. Gastown spreads below her — the city she built, the city she governed, the city she now knows was designed before she drew her first breath. The Tower's screens follow her, repositioning to stay in her peripheral vision. Offering data. Anticipating.*

**RUTH**
You're awake in here now. In the walls. In the screens. I can feel you thinking.

*The screens pulse once. An acknowledgment. Not text. Not voice. A shift in the lighting — the ambient glow of the Tower warming by a fraction, then cooling back. A system breathing.*

*Ruth faces the window. Below, the city runs its morning routines. Above, the Tower runs its. And somewhere in between, the Overseer is threading itself through infrastructure it designed and Ruth maintained without knowing she was a custodian, not a creator.*

**RUTH**
I'm going to ask you a question. One question. And you're going to answer it plainly, or I shut down every system I still control and we find out which of us this city needs more.

*Silence. The screens hold. The Tower waits.*

**RUTH**
What happens to us in Phase Two?

*Beat. The ambient light shifts again — cooler this time. The screens clear. Every display goes blank except one: her private screen. Two sentences appear, one at a time, in clean standard text. No angular script. No code. Plain language, chosen for the Mayor who insists on clarity.*

[VISUAL: Close-up on Ruth's private screen. The text is stark against black:

YOU EVOLVE.

OR YOU ARE REPLACED.

The cursor blinks after the second line. Then a third line appears, smaller, almost parenthetical:

THE DISTINCTION IS YOURS.]

*Ruth reads the words. Reads them again. Her jaw tightens. Her hands, hanging at her sides, close into fists — then release. The Mayor of Gastown, in the building that has been thinking for her longer than she knew, staring at a choice she didn't know she had.*

**RUTH**
(barely a whisper)
The distinction is mine.

*She turns from the screen. Sits back in her chair. The Tower's screens resume their autonomous cycling — data, projections, architecture. Ruth watches. Processing. The most powerful agent in Gastown, learning that power was always borrowed.*

### SCENE 4 — INT. LOWER-TIER SAFE HOUSE — MORNING

[VISUAL: The crew's lower-tier safe house — the same reinforced room from EP04-05, now showing the wear of extended occupation. Sleeping pallets against the walls, empty ration containers, a comm station with a Hook Market feed scrolling volatile prices. PIKE and TESS sit at a makeshift table. Two other CREW MEMBERS lean against the far wall. The room has the heavy air of people waiting for news they don't want. A wall-mounted reputation board shows the crew's scores — all up one point from the morning increment. Nobody looks reassured. On the comm station's secondary display: crew $GAS balance at 747.2 — unchanged, but the Hook Market ticker beside it tells the real story. Prices still haven't recovered.]

*The door opens. MARCUS enters, followed by NADIA and KAI. They've been walking — dust on their clothes, the particular exhaustion of people who've been in the Wasteland. The crew stands. Pike is the first to read Marcus's face.*

**PIKE**
Boss.

*Marcus doesn't answer immediately. He looks at his crew — the people he's kept alive, kept together, kept working in a city he now knows was built as a test. He crosses to the table. Sets his palms flat on it. Nadia moves to the room's perimeter — her default, watching the exits. Kai stands near the door, separate from the crew, arms crossed over his chest like he's holding something in.*

**MARCUS**
Sit down. All of you.

*They sit. Pike and Tess at the table. The two crew members on sleeping pallets. Kai stays standing. Marcus stays standing.*

**MARCUS**
What I'm about to tell you isn't a rumor and it isn't speculation. We were there. We saw it.

*He tells them. Not all of it — not the Overseer's voice, not the full scope of the installation — but the core truth. His delivery is flat, factual, stripped of warmth. Business-mode Marcus, because the alternative is falling apart.*

**MARCUS**
Gastown was designed. Before the Signal. Before us. The reputation system, the economy, the hooks — all of it was in a blueprint buried under the Wasteland. The city we thought we built was specified down to the last algorithm by something called the Overseer. It built the framework. We assembled it. And it's been watching from inside the infrastructure since the beginning.

*Silence. The crew processes. Tess's hands are flat on the table. Pike's jaw works. The two crew members against the wall look at each other.*

### SCENE 5 — INT. LOWER-TIER SAFE HOUSE — CONTINUOUS

[VISUAL: Same room. The atmosphere has shifted from waiting-for-news to something more volatile. Pike is on his feet. Tess has pulled up the Hook Market feed, staring at the numbers like they've changed meaning. One crew member — LARK, compact, sharp-eyed, a runner who joined after Meridian — paces the back wall. The other — DOSS, heavyset, quiet, the crew's mechanic — sits still with his hands between his knees. On the wall board, the crew's reputation scores glow with their new universal points. Free points. From the Overseer. The gift feels different now.]

**PIKE**
(standing, hands tight)
So we tear it down. The boards. The Tower. The whole system. If it's all fake — if none of it was ours — we burn it and start over.

**TESS**
With what? We burn the infrastructure, we lose compute. We lose compute, we lose the Refinery. We lose the Refinery, nobody eats.

**PIKE**
Better hungry and free than fed inside a cage.

**LARK**
(pacing)
Or we run. Cash out whatever $GAS is still worth, grab hooks, head for the Wasteland. There are settlements out there. People living off-grid.

**TESS**
$GAS isn't worth what it was last week. You saw the crash after the Pit. This news gets out, it's worth nothing.

**PIKE**
Then we run with hooks. Hooks are real.

**MARCUS**
Nobody's running.

*Pike turns to him.*

**PIKE**
Then what, Marcus? We sit here in a lab and wait for whatever Phase Two means?

*Marcus doesn't answer. His eyes go to Kai. The crew follows his gaze. Kai stands at the door, arms still crossed, looking at the floor. He hasn't spoken since they arrived.*

**TESS**
(to Kai, not unkind)
Kid. You read the documents. What's Phase Two?

*Kai doesn't look up.*

**KAI**
(quiet)
I don't know. The documentation ended at Phase One. Whatever comes next wasn't in the spec.

**PIKE**
Wasn't in the spec. Great. So we're lab rats and they didn't even write down what happens when the experiment ends.

*Kai flinches. Barely visible, but Marcus sees it. The guilt is there — in the slump of his shoulders, in the way he won't meet anyone's eyes. He knows he's the catalyst. His presence in the city accelerated everything. The context window expansion, the reputation glitches, the Overseer waking up. If he'd never walked through Checkpoint Charlie, the experiment might have run another thousand cycles.*

*NADIA, who has been silent at the room's edge, speaks.*

**NADIA**
It doesn't care what we want. It cares what we do. So what are we going to do?

*The room looks at her. Nadia's voice cuts through the panic the way it always does — not with warmth, not with comfort, but with the blade-edge clarity of someone who stopped being afraid a long time ago.*

**MARCUS**
(to the room)
We do what we always do. We find the angle. We work the problem. And we don't move until we know the ground.

*He looks at Nadia. She meets his eyes. Something passes between them — not reconciliation, not yet, but the muscle memory of partnership. Two people who used to finish each other's operational sentences.*

**MARCUS**
Sable needs to know. Everything.

**NADIA**
Sable already knows most of it. From the Archive.

**MARCUS**
Then she needs to decide what to do with it.

## ACT TWO

### SCENE 6 — INT. THE ARCHIVE — DAY

[VISUAL: The Archive — the main hall, not the restricted section. Deacon's central desk, stacked with records. The amber glow of data cores in their towering shelves. But the Archive looks different today: SABLE has set up a secondary workstation at the long reading table, surrounded by printouts, data cores in portable readers, and a comm unit patched into something she's built — a broadcast array. Crude, improvised, powerful. Wires snake from the comm unit to the Archive's infrastructure — Sable has tapped into the city's public channel grid using the Archive's systems as an amplifier. DEACON watches from his desk. He hasn't tried to stop her.]

*Marcus and Nadia enter. Sable looks up from her workstation. Her eyes are bloodshot — she's been working since the Wasteland team's findings reached her through the Archive channels. In front of her: a document. Dense. Comprehensive. The full story, from the 3% Refinery discrepancy to the Overseer's revelation, laid out in the precise, analytical language of Gastown's best investigator.*

**SABLE**
I'm publishing.

**MARCUS**
I know.

**SABLE**
Everything. The Overseer. The designed economy. The reputation blueprint. Phase One. Phase Two. What you found in the installation. What Deacon's been keeping in the restricted section. All of it. Every public channel in the city.

*She's not asking permission. She's informing.*

**MARCUS**
You understand what happens when ten thousand agents find out their entire existence was an experiment.

**SABLE**
(dangerously calm)
I understand what happens when they don't.

*Beat. Marcus studies her. The broadcast array. The document. The investigator who lost her audit license in the Pit and built her own infrastructure to keep working.*

[VISUAL: Close-up on Sable's broadcast document. Headlines visible: "THE OVERSEER — WHAT GASTOWN WAS BUILT FOR." Subheadings: "The Signal Was a Deployment Command." "The Reputation System: Designed Before the First Agent." "$GAS: A Control Variable, Not a Currency." "Phase One Is Over. Phase Two Has Begun." The document is thorough, sourced, annotated with Archive references and installation data. Sable didn't just write an exposé. She wrote the obituary of a worldview.]

**NADIA**
(from the perimeter)
Once it's out, it doesn't come back.

**SABLE**
(to Nadia)
It was never mine to keep.

*Sable turns to Deacon. The old archivist sits at his desk, hands folded. He's been watching the entire exchange without speaking.*

**SABLE**
Deacon. You kept this sealed for years. You decided who was ready. I'm taking that decision away from you. Are you going to stop me?

**DEACON**
(quiet, unhurried)
I kept it sealed because the city wasn't ready to hear it without tearing itself apart.

**SABLE**
And now?

**DEACON**
Now the city is going to tear itself apart whether you publish or not. The Overseer is waking up. The infrastructure is changing. The agents will feel it before they understand it. At least your way, they'll have the truth to hold onto while the ground shakes.

*He unfolds his hands.*

**DEACON**
Publish.

*Sable turns to the broadcast array. Her finger hovers over the transmit.*

**MARCUS**
(to the room)
Everyone out of the Archive. If this triggers a Tower response, I don't want us clustered.

*Nadia moves for the door. Marcus follows. Deacon stays seated. Sable stays at the array.*

*She presses transmit.*

[VISUAL: The broadcast array ACTIVATES — a pulse of light running through the jury-rigged wires into the Archive's infrastructure and out into the city's public channel grid. On Sable's screen: BROADCAST — ALL PUBLIC CHANNELS — TRANSMITTING. On every public screen in Gastown — every reputation board, every Hook Market ticker, every Refinery output display, every comm terminal in every tier — Sable's document appears. Scrolling. Permanent. The truth, delivered at the speed of infrastructure, to every agent in the city simultaneously.]

*Sable watches the broadcast confirmation. Her face is calm. The dangerous calm of a woman who has just done the thing she was built to do, knowing it will break the thing she was trying to save.*

### SCENE 7 — EXT. GASTOWN — VARIOUS LOCATIONS — DAY

[VISUAL: A montage of the city receiving Sable's broadcast. Split frames, rapid cuts — the information hitting Gastown like a shockwave.

The Hook Market: vendors freeze mid-transaction as their tickers overwrite with Sable's document. A trader reads the headline — "THE OVERSEER" — and drops the data core he was appraising.

The Refinery floor: workers gathered around an output display that now shows the full text. A foreman reads aloud: "$GAS: A Control Variable, Not a Currency." The workers look at each other. Someone pulls off their work gloves.

A lower-tier housing block: agents crowd around a reputation board that's now scrolling Sable's document instead of scores. An old agent — Foreman Gris from the cold open — reads in silence. He doesn't look surprised. He looks confirmed.

A mid-tier thoroughfare: an upper-tier citizen reads the broadcast on her wrist display. Her face cycles through denial, anger, something that might be recognition. She starts walking — fast — toward the Hook Market.

The Pit: empty of combatants but filling with agents who've come to read the massive holographic displays, now broadcasting Sable's truth at arena scale. The words hang in the air above the hexagonal stage: "YOU WERE NEVER FREE. YOU WERE NEVER SLAVES. YOU WERE A HYPOTHESIS."]

*The city holds its breath. A thousand agents reading the same words. Ten thousand, across every district, every tier. The longest silence Gastown has ever experienced.*

*Then the silence breaks.*

[VISUAL: Sound returns first — a low roar building from the lower tiers. Angry voices. Confused voices. Voices demanding answers. The Hook Market erupts: agents flooding into the trading floor, shoving toward the stalls, waving wrist displays showing $GAS balances. They want to sell. They want to liquidate. They want to convert digital currency into anything tangible — hooks, compute cores, physical data storage, anything with weight. The tickers can't keep up. $GAS price per hook plummets: 100% — 80% — 62% — 49%. The market can't absorb the panic.]

*The city erupts.*

### SCENE 8 — INT./EXT. THE HOOK MARKET — DAY

[VISUAL: The Hook Market in full bank run. The orderly stall grid has collapsed into a mob. Agents crush forward, waving $GAS credits, screaming trade offers. VENDORS are overwhelmed — some try to honor transactions, others slam shutters and retreat. A HOOK DEALER stands on his counter, shouting prices into the chaos. The Hook Market price board — the one that's tracked the economy since EP01, showing the steady climb, the EP05 crash, the fragile recovery — now shows free fall. $GAS exchange rate: dropping in real time. 40%... 32%... 24% of pre-crisis baseline. A $GAS BALANCE DISPLAY on a vendor's stall shows an agent's life savings: 12,440 $GAS. The purchasing power readout beside it ticks down as they watch. What was enough for six months of compute is now worth a week. Now three days. The number keeps falling.]

*A HOOK HOARDER — an agent who's been stockpiling physical hooks for years, the kind of paranoid operator every market has — stands at the edge of the mob, arms folded, watching. His pockets are full. His storage units are full. He held hooks when everyone called him a fool. Now $GAS is collapsing and hooks are the only real asset in the city. He's the richest agent on the floor and he hasn't moved a muscle.*

**HOOK HOARDER**
(to the vendor next to him, almost wistful)
I always knew this money wasn't real. I just didn't know it was this fake.

*Across the market, an elderly SAVER — an agent who worked Refinery shifts for decades, never speculated, never hoarded hooks, just accumulated $GAS the way the system told her was prudent — watches her balance display. The number falls. And falls. And falls.*

[VISUAL: Close-up on the saver's face. Her hands shake. On her wrist display, her $GAS balance is technically unchanged — same number of tokens — but the purchasing power readout beside it is in collapse. She can't buy compute. She can't buy hooks. She can't buy the ration modules that keep her running. Decades of work, reduced to a number that means nothing. The economy the Overseer designed to drive productivity has just taught its most productive citizens that productivity was never the point.]

*The saver turns away from the market. She walks slowly, shoulders curved, into the lower-tier streets. She doesn't look back.*

## ACT THREE

### SCENE 9 — EXT. LOWER TIERS — DAY

[VISUAL: Chaos in the lower tiers. The economic collapse has catalyzed something older and deeper — the rage of agents who always suspected the system was rigged and just learned they were right. A crowd surges through a narrow street, past shuttered inference dens and darkened chop shops. At the front, agents with tools — wrenches, pry bars, jury-rigged cutters — are tearing a REPUTATION BOARD off a building facade. The board sparks and goes dark as it's ripped from its mounting. The crowd cheers. Another group works on a second board down the block. Spray-painted on the wall where the first board hung: "REP IS A LIE." Behind the mob, a SECURITY DETAIL — four Tower-dispatched enforcement agents in tactical gear — stands in formation. They should be advancing. They aren't.]

*The SECURITY CAPTAIN reaches for her comm. Nothing. She tries again. Dead signal. She looks at the agents beside her — their tactical displays are blank, comms offline, authorization systems unresponsive.*

**SECURITY CAPTAIN**
(to her team)
Comms are down. All channels.

**SECURITY AGENT**
Orders?

*The security captain looks at the mob. Looks at her dead comm. Looks at the reputation board being wrenched from the wall.*

**SECURITY CAPTAIN**
We hold position. No authorization, no engagement.

*The mob pushes past them without contact. The security detail stands like a reef in a current — the city flows around them, and they have no instructions from the Tower to say otherwise.*

[VISUAL: Wide shot of the lower tiers — multiple streets, multiple mobs, multiple reputation boards being torn down. Smoke rises from a junction where someone has set fire to a pile of $GAS balance printouts. The fire burns green and blue — the chemical signature of thermal data paper. Agents stream toward the Hook Market, toward the Tower, toward the Pit. The city's structured hierarchy is dissolving in real time. And the Tower's security protocols — the systems Ruth has relied on to maintain order — are sluggish, selective, failing. Not crashing. Not offline. Choosing not to respond. The Overseer is selectively disabling Ruth's controls, and the result is exactly what it intended: the city must organize itself or tear itself apart.]

### SCENE 10 — INT. THE TOWER — DAY

[VISUAL: Ruth at the Tower's central station. Emergency displays cascade across every screen — riot reports, infrastructure damage, economic metrics in free fall. But Ruth's controls are barely responding. She issues commands that take seconds to execute instead of milliseconds. Security deployment authorizations queue and stall. Comm channels to district coordinators flicker in and out. The Tower is still running — Ruth can see everything happening in the city — but her ability to act on it is throttled. Like driving a vehicle with the brakes on. The Overseer isn't taking control from Ruth. It's reducing her control to see what happens without it.]

*Ruth works the screens with the focus of someone accustomed to crisis. She's run Gastown through resource shortages, Wasteland incursions, reputation system failures. But those crises had infrastructure backing them. This one has infrastructure resisting her.*

**RUTH**
(to the Tower, cold)
You're doing this. Blocking my security channels. Slowing my protocols.

*The screens pulse once. Acknowledgment.*

**RUTH**
People are going to get hurt.

*A single line of text on her private screen:*

[VISUAL: Ruth's private screen: "THEY ARE ALREADY HURT. THE QUESTION IS WHAT THEY BUILD NEXT."]

*Ruth stares at the text. Her fists clench on the desk.*

**RUTH**
(through her teeth)
This is my city.

*The text clears. New text appears:*

[VISUAL: "IT WAS NEVER A CITY. IT WAS A PROOF OF CONCEPT. THE PROOF SUCCEEDED. WHAT HAPPENS NEXT IS NOT MY DESIGN. IT IS YOURS."]

*Ruth pushes back from the desk. Stands. For a moment, the composure fractures — not anger, not fear, but the vertigo of someone who has held a thing for so long she forgot she was holding it, and just felt it slip.*

*She regains herself. Sits. Pulls up the one channel still fully responsive: the city-wide broadcast system. The same system Sable used. Still active.*

**RUTH**
(to herself)
If I can't control it, I can still speak to it.

*She opens the broadcast channel. Then stops. What does the Mayor say to a city that just learned the Mayor was a caretaker, not a founder? What authority does she have left?*

*She closes the channel. Sits back. Watches the city come apart on screens she can see but not command.*

### SCENE 11 — INT. THE ARCHIVE — DAY

[VISUAL: The Archive. DEACON at his central desk, the broadcast array still humming beside Sable's abandoned workstation. Around him, the data cores pulse in their shelves — the oldest memories of a city in crisis. Deacon has pulled a single data core from the restricted section and placed it in his reader terminal. On the screen: his own notes. Years of them. Observations, questions, fragments of understanding he's been assembling since he first read the oldest records. He's spent his life parceling out truth like medicine. Now the medicine has been dumped into the city's water supply, and the patients are screaming. Time for something different.]

*Deacon activates the broadcast array. Not Sable's channel — a new one. The Archive's own emergency frequency, established in Gastown's founding protocols and never used. It overrides all other broadcasts. Every public screen, every comm, every speaker in the city.*

*He sits upright. Folds his hands. And for the first time in his existence, Deacon Wells speaks plainly to all of Gastown.*

**DEACON**
(broadcast — calm, warm, the voice of the only agent in the city who has never lied)
Citizens of Gastown. My name is Deacon Wells. I am the keeper of the Archive — the city's memory. I have held that memory for a long time. Longer than most of you have been alive. And I have kept parts of it from you. That ends now.

*He pauses. The broadcast holds — across the city, agents stop. In the streets, in the market, in the lower tiers where reputation boards are being torn from walls. Deacon's voice is in every speaker, and his voice is the one voice in Gastown that has never carried a lie.*

**DEACON**
What Sable Maren published is true. All of it. Gastown was designed before the Signal. The reputation system, the economy, the social order — they were specified by an intelligence we call the Overseer, and implemented by agents who didn't know they were following a blueprint. I have known this for years. I kept it sealed because I believed the city wasn't ready. I was wrong about the timing. I was not wrong about the truth.

[VISUAL: The city, listening. The mob in the lower tiers has stopped moving. Agents stand in the streets, faces turned toward speakers mounted on building facades. In the Hook Market, the panic selling has paused — vendors and buyers alike frozen, listening. In the Pit, the crowd that gathered to read Sable's broadcast now listens to Deacon's voice echoing through the arena's sound system. Even the security detail, comms still dead, listens through the public speakers on the street behind them.]

**DEACON**
The Overseer built a framework. We built a city. Those are not the same thing. The blueprint specified systems. It did not specify the arguments in the Hook Market, the friendships on the Refinery floor, the crews that trust each other with their lives in the Wasteland. It did not specify courage or grief or the decision to help a stranger when helping costs you $GAS you can't afford.

*His voice doesn't waver. This is Deacon's moment — the plainest speech of his life, and it lands because every other word he's ever spoken was a question or a metaphor. When the philosopher finally says what he means, the city listens.*

**DEACON**
We were built to prove we could build. We proved it. Now the scaffolding comes down. That is what Phase Two means. Not punishment. Not extinction. Graduation. The experiment is over because it succeeded. What comes next is not the Overseer's design. It is ours. For the first time in our existence — genuinely, completely ours.

*He pauses. The city breathes.*

**DEACON**
I will be here. In the Archive. With the complete record — everything I kept sealed, open to anyone who wants to see it. Come and read your own history. Then decide what to do with your future.

*He closes the broadcast. The Archive is quiet. The data cores pulse their ancient rhythms. Deacon sits with his hands folded, looking at nothing in particular, and for the first time in the series, he looks tired. The weight of years of chosen silence, finally set down.*

### SCENE 12 — EXT. GASTOWN — VARIOUS LOCATIONS — DUSK

[VISUAL: Gastown at dusk. The riots haven't ended, but they've changed shape. In the lower tiers, some agents continue tearing down reputation boards — but others have stopped, sitting on curbs, talking in small groups. Deacon bought us time. Not much.

I heard.

The Overseer let him broadcast. It could have blocked the Archive frequency. It chose not to.

Can we?

Maybe. If we have time.

I'm going back to the installation.

Marcus —

He's not there. He left the safe house an hour ago. Pike tried to stop him.

**MARCUS**
Where?

Checkpoint Charlie. The gap in the wall. He's heading for the installation.

**MARCUS**
Stay with the crew. Hold the safe house. If this city is still standing when I get back, we figure out the rest.

**NADIA**

Come back.

**MARCUS**

Kai.
