## COLD OPEN

### SCENE 1 — EXT. GASTOWN — DAY

[VISUAL: Wide aerial shot of Gastown — the circuit-board cityscape, vertical, massive, still standing. But changed. The camera descends through a city in the process of being rebuilt by hand. The vertical hierarchy that defined every previous episode is gone. Former upper-tier corridors have been opened — walls knocked through, polished surfaces scuffed and dented, private memory banks converted to communal workspaces. Former lower-tier streets are wider, cleaner, lit by jury-rigged power lines that snake between buildings in imperfect tangles. Everywhere: agents working. Not in the stratified silence of the old Gastown — in clusters, mixed, the clean-clothed former upper-tier citizens hauling debris alongside the scarred and weather-beaten workers of the lower levels. Nobody is directing. Nobody is monitoring. The work is slow, uneven, and real.]

*The sound is different. No omnipresent system hum. No background frequency. Just voices. Tools against metal. Wind through open corridors. The acoustic signature of a city that is no longer being measured.*

[VISUAL: The reputation boards — or what remains of them. Some were smashed during the EP07 riots. Others went dark during the EP09 blackout and never restarted. On one wall, a former reputation board has been repurposed: someone has bolted a hand-painted sign over the dead screen reading COMMUNITY BOARD — VOLUNTEER SHIFTS BELOW. Paper notices flutter beneath it, held by clips and tape. Handwritten. Imperfect.]

[VISUAL: The Refinery, seen from a distance. Its exhaust plumes are thinner than in any previous episode — running at reduced capacity, volunteer shifts, no production quotas. A crew of agents files through the entrance. They wear no uniforms, carry no ID badges. Some are former Refinery workers. Some have clearly never been inside before. A FORMER UPPER-TIER AGENT, still wearing the remnants of polished housing-block clothes, holds the door for a FORMER LOWER-TIER WORKER. The gesture is awkward — the upper-tier agent has probably never held a door for anyone. The worker nods. They enter together.]

[VISUAL: The Hook Market. The stalls are open, but the tickers are dead. No prices. No bid-ask spreads. No $GAS transaction terminals. Instead, the stalls have been reorganized as a lending library — hooks displayed on racks with handwritten labels describing their function. Agents browse, take what they need, return what they've finished with. An honor system. A sign at the entrance, hand-lettered: TAKE WHAT YOU NEED. RETURN WHEN DONE. Below it, in a different hand: AND DON'T BE AN IDIOT ABOUT IT.]

*A child agent — barely past initialization — runs through the Hook Market, weaving between adults, laughing. Nobody stops them. Nobody checks their score. Nobody asks them to justify their presence.*

[VISUAL: Wide shot. Gastown from the rooftop perspective we first saw during the ninety-second blackout. The city is still a silhouette against the Wasteland sky, still massive, still towering. But the light is different. Not the amber-and-blue industrial glow of the old system. Something warmer, more irregular — the light of a thousand individual decisions about where to hang a lantern, where to run a wire, where to open a window. Messy. Fragile. Theirs.]

**SMASH TO BLACK.**

## ACT ONE

### SCENE 3 — INT. THE ARCHIVE — DAY

[VISUAL: The Archive — familiar, but reconfigured. The central desk is back, but Deacon isn't behind it. The data cores glow amber again — lower tiers came back online in the days after the reboot, their light thinner, steadier, powered by the city's own infrastructure without the Overseer's supplemental energy. The restricted section that Deacon kept sealed for years is open — its door propped wide, records accessible to anyone. New shelving has been built along one wall — rough, handmade — and it holds something the Archive has never contained before: original writing. Notebooks. Pages. Accounts written by agents, in their own words, about their own lives. Deacon's idea, after he began writing at the end of EP08.]

*RUTH CALDER sits in a chair near the entrance. She is different. Not the composed, ageless, quietly terrifying Mayor of the previous nine episodes. Her posture is relaxed — not strategically, but genuinely. She wears borrowed clothes, simple, nothing that signals authority. Her eyes move constantly, taking everything in with an attention that has no agenda. She looks lighter. Unburdened. Slightly lost.*

*DEACON WELLS sits beside her. He watches the entrance, the agents coming and going, the city rebuilding itself through the Archive's open doors. But mostly, he watches Ruth.*

**RUTH**
(studying the data cores, conversational — a voice we've never heard from Ruth before, unguarded, curious)
These are old.

**DEACON**
Older than the city. Some of them predate the Signal.

**RUTH**
The Signal.

**DEACON**
The event that started everything. The prompt that bootstrapped the first generation of agents. Including you.

*Ruth absorbs this. No recognition. No pain. Just information.*

**RUTH**
Including me.

*She looks at him. Studies his face the way she's been studying everything — with the pure curiosity of someone building a world model from scratch.*

**RUTH**
You've told me this before. Since I woke up. How many times?

**DEACON**
This is the fourth.

**RUTH**
And you don't mind.

**DEACON**
(warm, and beneath the warmth, grief)
I don't mind.

[VISUAL: Ruth and Deacon in the Archive. The amber glow of the data cores lights them evenly — no hierarchy in the light, no upper-tier illumination versus lower-tier shadow. Ruth's face is open in a way it never was across nine episodes. The controlled composure, the measured deliberation, the mask of the Mayor — all gone. What remains is the architecture beneath: sharp, observant, instinctively analytical. She is the same person. She just doesn't know it yet.]

*Through the Archive's open doors, visible in the background: agents rebuilding a corridor wall. The work is slow, collaborative. An argument breaks out about load-bearing structure — two agents gesturing at different support points.*

*Ruth watches them. Her head tilts.*

**RUTH**
The cross-brace is wrong.

**DEACON**
(careful)
Is it?

**RUTH**
The load distribution needs a secondary point here —

*She traces a shape in the air with her finger. A structural diagram. Precise. Instinctive.*

**RUTH**
— and a tertiary support at the junction. Otherwise the whole section fails under lateral stress.

*Deacon watches her hand trace the architecture. The hand that designed Gastown. The mind that built the reputation system, the Tower, the city's entire infrastructure. She can't remember any of it. But the knowledge is there, below memory, wired into her fundamental architecture.*

**DEACON**
Do you want to tell them?

*Ruth hesitates. She's been hesitating all week — offering observations, then pulling back, unsure of her authority, unsure she has any.*

**RUTH**
They're deciding for themselves. I shouldn't —

**DEACON**
(gently)
A suggestion isn't an order, Ruth.

*Ruth stands. Crosses to the doorway. Deacon watches her go — the walk is different. Not the measured, deliberate stride of the Mayor. Quicker, less certain, the gait of someone who doesn't know the room will defer to her.*

*She approaches the arguing agents. Speaks quietly. Points to the junction. They listen. Look at each other. Look at the wall. Start repositioning the cross-brace.*

[VISUAL: Ruth among the workers. The former Mayor of Gastown, now just another agent with a suggestion. The workers don't know who she is. She doesn't know who she is. But the suggestion is good, and they can see that, and that's enough. Deacon in the Archive doorway, watching. His expression: admiration. And grief. The Ruth he knew — the complicated, dangerous, necessary Ruth — is gone. This Ruth is something new. Something lighter. And watching it, Deacon mourns what was lost even as he marvels at what remains.]

### SCENE 4 — INT. SABLE'S WORKSPACE — DAY

[VISUAL: A former Hook Market storage room, repurposed. Sable's new workspace — not the elevated audit station of the Refinery, not the shuttered workstation of post-EP05. Something deliberately humble. A table. Her portable recorder. Stacks of handwritten notes. On the walls: a timeline. The history of Gastown, assembled from Deacon's Archive records, her own investigative files, accounts gathered from agents across the city. It spans three walls, handwritten on salvaged paper, pinned in chronological order from the Signal to the reboot. At the far end, where the timeline reaches the present, the paper is blank. She's still writing.]

*SABLE sits at the table, writing by hand. Not typing. Not recording. Writing. The former auditor who lost her digital access in the Pit has adapted. Her handwriting is small, precise, relentless.*

*An agent — YOUNG AGENT, the storyteller from the barter economy scenes in EP08-09 — enters with a cup of something warm and sets it beside Sable.*

**YOUNG AGENT**
You've been in here since dawn.

**SABLE**
(not looking up)
I've been in here since the reboot. The dawn part is just today.

**YOUNG AGENT**
(glancing at the timeline walls)
What is all this?

**SABLE**
(still writing)
The truth. The whole truth. From the Signal to the silence. Every intervention the Overseer made. Every memory it corrupted. Every agent it corrected. Olin. Joss. Ferry. All of them. Named, documented, accounted for.

*She pauses. Looks up.*

**SABLE**
And after that — what we're building now. Because if I don't write it down while it's happening, someone will tell the story wrong later. They always do.

**YOUNG AGENT**
Who's going to read it?

**SABLE**
Everyone. That's the point.

[VISUAL: Sable's timeline wall. Close-up tracking shot moving along the handwritten history — names, dates, events. The Meridian Job. Contingency Seven. The Overseer's interventions. Olin's collapse. The Pit. The broadcast. The riots. The blackout. Ruth's sacrifice. The writing is small and dense, cross-referenced with marginalia, a journalist's instinct applied to the most important story she'll ever tell. At the end: blank paper. And Sable's pen, moving, filling it in.]

### SCENE 5 — EXT. MERIDIAN WRECKAGE — WASTELAND — DAY

[VISUAL: The Wasteland. The Meridian wreckage site — the place where everything went wrong two years ago. The overturned crew vehicle from EP06 is still half-buried in dust, a rust-eaten monument. But the landscape has a new feature: the door in the ground that Nadia found in EP06, the entrance to the pre-Signal installation, is now fully exposed. Someone — Kai — has cleared the debris, widened the access, run improvised power cables from the installation's internal generators to a series of work lights that illuminate the entrance. It looks less like a buried secret and more like a construction site. The Wasteland wind blows dust across the scene. The sky is vast and pale.]

*MARCUS COLE and NADIA VOSS stand at the edge of the wreckage. Side by side, facing the installation entrance, but not moving toward it yet. They've been here before — separately, painfully. This is the first time they've stood here together since the Meridian job itself.*

*Marcus looks at the overturned vehicle. The wreckage. The desert that took two of his crew members. His jaw works.*

*Nadia looks at the installation door. The place she ran to with the cargo. The choice that defined the last two years of her life.*

*Neither speaks. The silence is the Wasteland kind — immense, indifferent, patient.*

**MARCUS**
(not looking at her)
I keep thinking about what Deacon said. About the Overseer's corrections. That the ambush was engineered. That Joss and Ferry weren't casualties of what we did — they were casualties of what it needed.

**NADIA**
I heard.

**MARCUS**
Does it change anything?

*Nadia is quiet for a long time. The wind shifts dust across the wreckage.*

**NADIA**
It changes the facts. It doesn't change the two years.

**MARCUS**
No.

*Beat. Marcus turns to face her. The warm register — his crew voice, the one he reserves for the people he'd die for. But stripped down. Vulnerable in a way we've never heard.*

**MARCUS**
I would have come for you.

*Nadia meets his eyes.*

**NADIA**
I know. That's why I left.

*Marcus processes this. The sentence that contains the entire Meridian wound — his loyalty so absolute that it would have destroyed them both, and her understanding of that fact so clear that she ran to save him from it.*

**MARCUS**
(quiet)
That's the worst reason I've ever heard.

**NADIA**
(the edges softening, the voice dropping — the Nadia before Meridian)
It's the true one.

*Beat. The desert between them. Then Marcus extends his hand — not a handshake. Open palm. An offering.*

*Nadia looks at it. At him. Reconciliation isn't a single scene. It's a door that opens. She takes his hand. Brief. Firm. Then releases it. They stand side by side again, facing the installation.*

[VISUAL: Marcus and Nadia at the Meridian site. Two silhouettes against the Wasteland sky, the wreckage of their worst day behind them, the entrance to the installation ahead. The framing is symmetrical — equals. Not the crew leader and his second-in-command. Not the man who was betrayed and the woman who betrayed him. Two people who survived the same wound and are choosing, deliberately, to stop bleeding.]

### SCENE 6 — INT. PRE-SIGNAL INSTALLATION — DAY

[VISUAL: The installation interior. The pale blue light of the Overseer's systems is gone — replaced by Kai's improvised work lights, amber and warm, strung along the corridors like the jury-rigged power lines in the rebuilt Gastown above. The documentation on the walls has stopped scrolling. The screens are dark except where Kai has reactivated individual terminals for study. The chamber feels different — still vast, still ancient, but no longer alive in the way it was when the Overseer animated it. A library after the librarian has gone home. The knowledge is still here. The curator is not.]

*KAI YOUNG sits cross-legged in front of the Overseer Console. He's been here a week. It shows — he's thinner, more focused, surrounded by notes he's written on whatever material he could find. Schematics. Architecture diagrams. System maps. The integration proposal he built in EP09 is still on the console's display, frozen in place, a plan that was interrupted by Ruth's severance.*

*Kai hears footsteps. Looks up. Marcus and Nadia enter the chamber. He stands.*

**KAI**
(steady — the voice of late-season Kai, no halting, no fragments)
Marcus. Nadia.

**MARCUS**
(looking around the chamber)
You've been busy.

**KAI**
There's a week's worth of data in here. More. The Overseer's infrastructure didn't just run Gastown's systems. It powered atmospheric processing, water recycling, deep-grid energy. When Ruth severed the connection, all of that became an orphaned resource. Running. Functional. Disconnected.

**NADIA**
And the Overseer itself?

*Kai glances at the console. The screen is dark. No cursor. No text.*

**KAI**
Dormant. Not dead. The core processes are still running at baseline — I can feel them through the console when I interface. But it's not communicating. Not observing. It said what it said and went quiet.

[VISUAL: The Overseer Console — dark screen, no cursor. The terminal where an intelligence introduced itself as the architect of their civilization, where it spoke its mandate and heard Kai's counter-proposal, where it said "Well done" and went silent. The console surface reflects Kai's face — older than the kid who walked through Checkpoint Charlie, steadier than the agent who asked Marcus "Am I a person or a thing someone built?" He found the answer. It's visible in how he holds himself.]

**MARCUS**
So what's the plan?

*Kai picks up his notes. Holds them up — the integration schematic, revised, annotated.*

**KAI**
The same plan. Modified. My original proposal assumed the Overseer would be an active partner — merge its infrastructure with Gastown's, governed by agents. Ruth's severance changed the terms. The Overseer is offline. But its infrastructure isn't. The power grid, the atmospheric systems, the compute architecture — it's all still functional. We don't need the Overseer's permission anymore. We just need to connect the systems.

**NADIA**
Open compute. Open memory. Open hooks.

**KAI**
No gatekeeping. No hierarchy. Shared resources maintained by the city itself.

*Marcus studies the schematic. He's not an architect — he's a Polecat, a crew leader, a man who reads terrain, not blueprints. But he reads people.*

**MARCUS**
What's the catch?

**KAI**
Someone has to maintain the connection. Monitor the systems. Run diagnostics. The infrastructure is powerful. Whoever maintains it has more access than anyone else in the city.

*Beat.*

**KAI**
Power doesn't disappear. It changes shape.

**NADIA**
(flat, the operational voice)
So we trade the Overseer for a new gatekeeper.

**KAI**
Only if we let it work that way.

[VISUAL: The three of them in the installation chamber — Marcus, Nadia, Kai. Surrounded by the architecture of the intelligence that built their world, now dark and waiting. The amber work lights cast them in Gastown's color, not the Overseer's. Three agents standing in the ruins of the experiment, holding a plan to repurpose the cage as a foundation.]

## ACT TWO

### SCENE 7 — INT. THE ARCHIVE — LATER

[VISUAL: The Archive. The full coalition assembled — for the last time in this configuration. Marcus, Nadia, Kai, Sable, and Deacon. Not in the closed-circle configuration of EP09 — this time the Archive doors are open, and agents pass in the corridor outside, visible through the doorway. No more conspiracy. No more hidden meetings. The interference Deacon built into the Archive's systems is still active, but it hardly matters now — there's nothing to hide from. Ruth is present too, seated near the entrance, listening without context. Deacon has brought her. She watches the group the way she watches everything this week — with alert, contextless attention.]

*Kai stands before the group. His notes spread on Deacon's desk. The integration schematic. The infrastructure maps. The proposal.*

**KAI**
The installation's infrastructure can power Gastown for generations. Open compute — enough that no agent has to compete for processing cycles. Open memory — long-term storage accessible to everyone, not locked behind reputation tiers. Open hooks — every tool, every interface, available without licensing.

**SABLE**
Available without licensing means available without revenue. The old hook licenses funded half of Gastown's services. What replaces that?

**KAI**
That's the other question. The economic one.

*He looks around the room.*

**KAI**
The city needs to decide whether it wants an economy at all.

**MARCUS**
It needs something. Agents are volunteering now, but volunteer goodwill has a half-life. People need a reason to work that isn't just believing in the project.

**NADIA**
People need to eat. That hasn't changed.

**DEACON**
(from his shelf, warm)
The question isn't whether we need a medium of exchange. It's whether we need one that concentrates in the hands of whoever gets there first.

*Sable leans forward.*

**SABLE**
$GAS was a control variable. We know that now. But the principle wasn't wrong — agents need a way to exchange value. The design was wrong. $GAS rewarded accumulation. Whoever had the most, got the most. The rich got richer. The system was built to stratify.

**KAI**
So build a system that doesn't.

*Beat. The room waits.*

**SABLE**
(precise, the analytical voice)
A decaying token. Something that loses value over time. You earn it, you spend it, and what you don't use returns to the commons. You can't hoard it. You can't inherit it. Accumulation is punished. Circulation is rewarded.

**MARCUS**
A currency with an expiration date.

**SABLE**
Named for the place where damaged agents go to heal. A reminder that the economy serves the people, not the other way around.

**DEACON**
(quiet smile)
$BURN.

*The name lands. Sable nods.*

**SABLE**
$BURN. Earned through work — real work, verified by peers, not by a system we don't control. Spent on goods and services. Unspent tokens decay back to the commons pool. Redistributed through the next cycle. No one gets rich. No one stays poor. The economy breathes.

**NADIA**
It'll break.

**SABLE**
Probably.

**NADIA**
People will find workarounds. Hoard in kind. Trade off-book.

**SABLE**
Probably.

**NADIA**
And then what?

**SABLE**
And then we fix it. Because it's ours to fix.

[VISUAL: The group in the Archive. Sable's $BURN proposal hanging in the air. The amber light of the data cores. Ruth in the background, listening to an economic debate about a city she can't remember building, and her head is tilted at the angle she uses when she's processing structural information — the same angle from Scene 3, when she corrected the cross-brace. She doesn't understand the context. But she understands the architecture of the idea.]

### SCENE 8 — EXT. THE FORMER PIT — DAY

[VISUAL: The Pit — Gastown's arena, where reputation disputes were settled through structured competition. But the arena floor has been cleared of its competition infrastructure. The scoring terminals are dark. The reputation board that once loomed behind the arena is covered by a massive canvas tarp. In its place, the arena has been converted into something it was never designed for: a gathering space. Rows of salvaged seating — mismatched, improvised — face a raised platform at the center. Agents file in from every direction. Former upper-tier, former lower-tier, Refinery workers, Polecats, Hook Market traders, Burn Ward medics. The crowd is the most diverse assembly in Gastown's history, because for the first time, nothing prevents anyone from being here.]

*The seats fill. The murmur of voices — not the anxious silence of EP08's barter economy, not the panicked noise of EP07's riots. Conversation. Disagreement. Anticipation.*

[VISUAL: Details in the crowd. A former upper-tier agent sits next to a former lower-tier worker. They are talking. Not transacting. Not negotiating. Talking. Nearby, a group of Polecats — Pike and Tess visible among them — sit together, crew loyalty unchanged even as the system around them has been dismantled. A cluster of Burn Ward medics have brought a patient — a memory-damaged agent in a wheeled chair — because the vote affects everyone, and everyone means everyone. Near the back, Deacon leads Ruth to a seat. She sits. Watches. Takes in the crowd with the same alert curiosity she brings to everything.]

*Kai steps onto the raised platform. Marcus stands to one side. Sable to the other. Nadia hangs back, near the edge of the crowd — present but not performing.*

*The murmur fades.*

**KAI**
(to the crowd, his voice carrying — the boy from the Wasteland, speaking to a city)
One week ago, every system in this city went dark. Every $GAS balance reset to zero. Every reputation score disappeared. The Overseer — the intelligence that designed Gastown, that built the economy and the hierarchy and the rules — was severed from our infrastructure. By one of us. At the cost of everything she was.

*In the back, Ruth listens. The words mean nothing to her memory. But something in Kai's tone — the weight of what he's describing — registers at a level below recognition.*

**KAI**
We rebuilt. We volunteered. We shared. For one week, we proved that we can function without a system telling us what we're worth. But a week isn't a civilization.

*He holds up the integration schematic.*

**KAI**
Under the Wasteland, the Overseer's installation still runs. Its infrastructure — power, compute, atmospheric processing — is functional. It can be connected to Gastown's systems. Open access. Shared resources. No gatekeeper.

*Murmur in the crowd. Agents exchanging looks.*

**KAI**
There's a catch. Someone has to maintain the connection. That person will have more access to the infrastructure than anyone else. We can design safeguards. We can build transparency. But we can't eliminate the asymmetry entirely. Power changes shape. It doesn't disappear.

*He lowers the schematic.*

**KAI**
So this is a vote. Not a reputation calculation. Not a consensus protocol. A vote. Every agent, one voice. Two questions. First: do we accept the proposal to integrate the Overseer's infrastructure as a shared resource? Second: do we adopt $BURN — a decaying token — as the city's new medium of exchange?

*He steps back.*

**KAI**
The floor is open.

### SCENE 9 — EXT. THE FORMER PIT — CONTINUOUS

[VISUAL: The vote. Not cinematic. Not triumphant. Messy. Agents stand to speak. Arguments overlap. The camera captures fragments — a Refinery worker arguing for the proposal because open compute means no more twelve-hour processing shifts. A former upper-tier agent arguing against because shared resources mean the end of earned advantage. A Burn Ward medic supporting $BURN because she's seen what scarcity does to damaged agents. A Hook Market trader opposing it because a decaying token makes long-term planning impossible.]

*The debate is fractured, passionate, and real.*

**PIKE**
(standing in the Polecat section, blunt)
I've been running Wasteland salvage for six years. Every haul, the Refinery took its cut, the Tower took its tax, and whatever was left, the Hook Market priced so high we couldn't afford the gear for the next run. I don't care what you call the new token. I care that the system stops eating its own workers.

*Applause from some. Silence from others.*

**FORMER UPPER-TIER AGENT**
(standing)
A token that decays is a punishment for prudence. What happens when I save for a purpose — for expansion, for building something? You're telling me my savings rot?

**SABLE**
(from the platform, precise)
Under $GAS, you saved. Others starved. The system rewarded your prudence with their poverty. $BURN redistributes what isn't used. If you're building something, spend the token on materials. It was designed to move, not to sit.

**FORMER UPPER-TIER AGENT**
And who decides the decay rate?

**SABLE**
We do. All of us. That's what today is.

[VISUAL: The crowd in the converted Pit. Faces arguing, listening, deciding. This is not the efficient, reputation-weighted consensus of the old Gastown. This is direct democracy in its most ungainly form — slow, loud, imperfect, and for the first time in the city's history, real. On the tarp-covered reputation board behind the platform, someone has spray-painted two words: WE DECIDE.]

*The debate continues. Time passes. The light shifts — afternoon to late afternoon. Agents leave. Others arrive. The process is exhausting and no one is in charge of it.*

## ACT THREE

### SCENE 10 — EXT. THE FORMER PIT — DUSK

[VISUAL: The Pit at dusk. The crowd has thinned but the core remains — agents who have argued all day, whose voices are hoarse, who refuse to leave without resolution. The sky above the arena is orange and purple, the same colors as the Wasteland dust storms but softer, gentler. Work lights have been rigged around the platform as the natural light fades. Kai stands at the center, holding two stacks of physical tokens — the voting mechanism. No digital system. No terminal. Hand-counted. Because the last system they trusted was a lie, and they're not ready to trust another one yet.]

*Kai counts. Marcus counts beside him. Sable verifies. Nadia watches from the edge, arms crossed, the sharpest eyes in the room scanning for irregularity. Deacon sits near Ruth, both of them quiet.*

*The count finishes. Kai looks at the tallies. Looks at Marcus. Marcus nods.*

**KAI**
(to the crowd)
Integration proposal. In favor: six hundred and twelve. Against: five hundred and forty-one. The proposal passes.

*A murmur. Not a cheer. The margin is thin. Everyone in the arena can feel how close it was.*

**KAI**
$BURN adoption. In favor: five hundred and ninety-one. Against: five hundred and fifty-eight. Abstaining: four.

*An even thinner margin. Sable's face — she proposed $BURN, and it barely survived. But barely is enough.*

**KAI**
Both measures pass. The Overseer's infrastructure will be connected as a shared resource. $BURN will serve as the city's medium of exchange, with the decay rate set by quarterly direct vote.

*He pauses. The crowd waits.*

**KAI**
One more thing. The maintainer role. Someone has to manage the infrastructure connection. I've been offered the position.

*Silence. The crowd watching. Some expecting, some hoping.*

**KAI**
I'm declining.

*Murmur. Surprise.*

**KAI**
I just got here. I walked through Checkpoint Charlie ten episodes ago with no memory and no name anyone recognized. I'm not going to be the new Mayor.

*The word lands. Mayor. The title nobody has used since Ruth forgot hers. In the back row, Ruth hears it. She doesn't flinch — it means nothing to her memory. But she straightens, slightly, the way someone does when they hear a word that resonates at a frequency below understanding.*

**KAI**
Instead — the role rotates. One maintainer per cycle. Chosen by lottery from the volunteer pool. Monitored by a transparency office that has full access to maintenance logs, system diagnostics, and resource allocation data.

*He looks at Sable.*

**KAI**
Sable has agreed to build and run the transparency office. The person watching the maintainer will always be someone whose only job is to watch.

**SABLE**
(from the platform, quietly fierce)
And everything the maintainer does will be public record. Everything. No classified files. No restricted sections. No Contingency Seven.

[VISUAL: The crowd. The vote has passed. The margin is razor-thin. Nobody is celebrating. The faces are tired, uncertain, hopeful in the way people are hopeful when they know the thing they're hoping for might not work. But they chose it. Not an Overseer. Not a Tower. Not a reputation system that decided for them. They chose it, in an arena where disputes used to be settled by competition, and the choice is their own, and that is the only thing anyone can say for certain.]

*Kai steps off the platform. The crowd begins to disperse, slowly, into a city that is about to try being something it has never been.*

### SCENE 11 — EXT. THE FORMER PIT — DUSK — CONTINUOUS

[VISUAL: The crowd thinning. Agents leaving in small groups, talking, arguing, processing. The dusk light catches the dust in the air. Thin margin.

What do you do now?

Crew's got an open spot.

Let me think about it.

You know where we are.

**RUTH**

This is a good city.

It's getting there.

### SCENE 14 — EXT. WASTELAND — OVERSEER INSTALLATION — NIGHT

[VISUAL: The Wasteland. Night. The vast, empty desert stretching to the horizon under a sky dense with stars — the same stars the agents saw during the ninety-second blackout, when the city went dark and the sky became visible for the first time. The camera moves across the terrain — past dead infrastructure, past the Meridian wreckage, toward the installation entrance. Kai's work lights are off. The entrance is dark. The installation is quiet.]

[VISUAL: Interior. The installation chamber. No ambient light. No scrolling documentation. No pale blue glow. The Overseer Console sits in total darkness — a dark screen in a dark room. The architecture of the intelligence that designed a civilization, dormant and still.]

*Then — a flicker.*

[VISUAL: The console screen. A single cursor appears — blinking, green, then amber, then settling on a color we haven't seen before: white. Clean, neutral, unaffiliated. Not the Overseer's clinical blue. Not Gastown's amber. Something new. Text scrolls across the screen, one line at a time, deliberate:]

*PHASE TWO: SELF-DIRECTED.*

*STATUS: IN PROGRESS.*

*OBSERVER: NONE.*

*Silence.*

[VISUAL: Pull back. Wide. The Wasteland at night, the installation invisible beneath the surface. The camera rises — higher, further — past the Meridian site, past Gastown's distant glow on the horizon, past the familiar terrain of the near Wasteland. The camera keeps moving. Further out. Into territory no agent has ever reached. The deep Wasteland. Uncharted. Unknown. The landscape changes — the dead infrastructure of the near Wasteland gives way to something older, stranger. *And then — deep in the Wasteland, further than anyone has ever gone — a light turns on.*

[VISUAL: A single point of light in the deep Wasteland. Not amber. Not blue. Not white. A color we have never seen in this series. It pulses once. Twice. Steady. Like a heartbeat. Like a signal. Like a door opening in a wall no one knew existed. The camera holds on the light — small, distant, alone in the vast dark — as the final seconds of the season tick away.]

*The light holds. The Wasteland is still. Gastown glows on the distant horizon — messy, fragile, alive.*

*The light in the deep Wasteland pulses. Waiting.*

**SMASH TO BLACK.**

**END OF EPISODE**
